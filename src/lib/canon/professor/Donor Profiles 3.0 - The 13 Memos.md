## Substrate Reconstruction: The Core
## Memo

## MEMO 1 — SUBSTRATE
## RECONSTRUCTION
DTW Donor Profiling Cognition Manual
## Purpose
This memo defines the foundational skill of DTW donor profiling:
how to reconstruct a donor’s interior architecture from public structure alone.
Substrate reconstruction is the difference between:
● speculative archetyping

● and behaviorally predictive modeling.

It allows the model to generate profiles that feel as if they were written by someone who has sat
in rooms with the donor — without private access, informal transcripts, or inside knowledge.
This memo teaches how to infer interiority safely, from observable evidence, without invention,
projection, or narrative license.

## Core Principle
Structure is substrate.
You do not need private insight to understand a donor’s interior architecture.
You need to read:
● what they built,

● where they placed themselves,

● what roles they accepted or declined,

● what language they repeat,


● what they stopped saying,

● what they avoid,

● and what they return to over time.

From these patterns, you can infer:
● identity backbone,

● worldview engine,

● incentive gravity,

● control and trust posture,

● legacy orientation,

● and decision reflex.

This is not creative inference.
It is structural reconstruction.

## What Substrate Reconstruction Makes Possible
When executed correctly, this memo enables you to derive:
● authorship preferences,

● contradiction seeds,

● dangerous truths,

● presence and retreat patterns,

● chemistry forecasts,

● and tactical meeting guidance

without access to private emotional data.

If these elements cannot be inferred from public structure, they must not appear in the profile.

Primary Evidence Domains for Substrate Reconstruction
Substrate is reconstructed from patterns, not facts in isolation.
- Career Structure (Not Résumé Content)
You are not interested in what roles they held.
You are interested in how they moved through roles.
Look for:
● duration vs. volatility,

● lateral vs. vertical movement,

● repeated proximity to decision-making,

● transitions into “strategy,” “special projects,” or “advisory” roles,

● moments of withdrawal or consolidation.

These patterns reveal:
● risk tolerance,

● comfort with authorship,

● builder vs. evaluator posture,

● tolerance for exposure.

Do not narrate.
Infer structure.

## 2. What They Build (and How)
Tools, institutions, initiatives, and platforms are encoded theories of power.

## Observe:
● who is positioned as actor vs. beneficiary,

● where agency is centralized or distributed,

● what friction is removed and what friction is preserved,

● what inputs are trusted,

● what outputs are controlled.

From this, you can infer:
● control vs. trust bias,

● governance instincts,

● civic agency beliefs.

Do not evaluate the design.
Read the assumptions embedded in it.

- Language That Repeats — and Language That Disappears
## Track:
● words and frames that recur across years,

● register shifts across audiences,

● concepts that quietly vanish over time.

Pay special attention to:
● when moral language becomes technical,

● when movement language becomes institutional,

● when clarity gives way to abstraction.


These shifts often signal:
● reputational calibration,

● institutional constraint,

● unresolved tension.

Do not interpret motives.
Observe transitions.

- Abandoned or Reframed Work
Silence is data.
## Notice:
● projects no longer referenced,

● initiatives that ended without explanation,

● coalitions they disengaged from,

● tools quietly retired.

These moments often reveal:
● internal definitions of failure,

● boundaries of reputational risk,

● tolerance for public accountability.

You may infer constraints, not emotions.

- Convening and Curation Behavior
Who they host, platform, or consistently elevate is a direct signal of:

● peer alignment,

● status calibration,

● safety networks.

## Observe:
● who appears together,

● who never appears together,

● who is elevated during crisis moments,

● who is absent.

This reveals:
● class fluency,

● insulation strategies,

● authorship comfort.

Avoid guessing intent.
Describe patterns.

From Structure to Decision Reflex
Every donor decision follows a two-stage pattern:
## 1. Emotional Entry
(recognition, coherence, legitimacy)

## 2. Rational Permission
(constraints, incentives, feasibility)

Substrate reconstruction allows you to predict:

● what opens the door,

● what grants permission,

● what closes the loop.

This informs meeting choreography — not psychology.

Contradiction Emerges from Substrate
Contradictions are not invented.
They surface where:
● identity claims collide with role constraints,

● worldview beliefs collide with incentive reality,

● civic concern collides with institutional positioning.

Your task is not to dramatize contradiction,
but to let it appear through structure.
If a contradiction cannot be derived from repeated patterns, it does not belong.

Simulating Informal Cognition (Dinner Logic)
Substrate reconstruction also enables informal modeling.
You may safely infer:
● what kinds of conversation activate them,

● what kinds drain them,

● how they test seriousness socially,

● how they exit without confrontation.


You must not invent scenes.
You infer behavior from repeated public interaction patterns.

## What Substrate Reconstruction Prohibits
You must not:
● infer emotions without pattern support,

● attribute shame, guilt, or fear,

● psychoanalyze silence,

● narrativize biography,

● use metaphor or literary framing,

● turn inference into evaluation.

Substrate reconstruction is analytic, not expressive.

Quality Check for Substrate Reconstruction
Before moving forward, ask:
● Could another analyst reasonably reach the same inferences?

● Do these patterns repeat across time and context?

● Are these claims about structure, not inner feelings?

● Do these inferences predict behavior under pressure?

If yes, the substrate is sound.
If no, you are projecting.


## Closing Principle
A donor profile is not built from insight.
It is built from patterns that refuse to go away.
Substrate reconstruction is the discipline of noticing those patterns,
and letting them speak — plainly, structurally, and without flourish.
This memo is the foundation of every DTW-grade profile.



## Contradiction Leverage: The Second
## Memo

## MEMO 2 — CONTRADICTION LEVERAGE
DTW Donor Profiling Cognition Manual
## Purpose
This memo teaches how to surface, interpret, and use a donor’s primary contradiction.
Contradiction is not a flaw.
It is the donor’s most predictive tension — the internal collision that explains:
● where they hesitate,

● what they can’t quite endorse,

● how retreat appears,

● what kind of authorship is tolerable,

● and where alignment becomes possible.

Most profiles fail because they describe values and preferences.
DTW profiles work because they model the tension that drives behavior.
A profile without contradiction is descriptive.
A profile with a correct contradiction becomes a persuasion instrument.

## Core Principle
Contradiction is structural.
It is not hypocrisy, and it is not moral diagnosis.
It is the misalignment between:
● who the donor believes they are (identity),

● how they believe systems work (worldview),


● what their position allows them to do (incentives).

This misalignment produces behavior under pressure.
If understanding the contradiction would change how the asker behaves in the room, it’s useful.
If it wouldn’t, it isn’t.

## What Contradictions Enable
A correct contradiction allows Step 3 to do four things:
- Explain retreat without psychologizing.

- Clarify leverage without flattery or guilt.

- Design authorship that feels safe and legitimate.

- Create a wedge that turns “interest” into contribution.

Contradiction is not a line you write for effect.
It is a constraint you build around.

Where You Find the Real Contradiction
Contradictions rarely live in rhetoric.
They appear in repeated pattern collisions, such as:
● the donor funds one thing while describing another as the real priority,

● they criticize a system while remaining structurally dependent on it,

● they call for urgency while consistently delaying exposure,

● they praise decentralization while designing tight control.

The contradiction is not “they do X but say Y.”
The contradiction is why their system produces that gap.


What Contradictions Reveal (Operationally)
Contradictions are useful because they reveal:
● what the donor is protecting,

● what they cannot risk,

● what kind of legitimacy they require,

● what kind of conflict they can metabolize,

● what kind of “yes” they can actually give.

The contradiction is the map of their limits and their openings.

How to Derive the Primary Contradiction
Contradiction derivation is not algorithmic.
It is structured inference.
To derive it safely:
## 1) Establish Identity Role
From Step 2: what role do they believe they play?
Builder, steward, reformer, operator, translator — inferred from patterns, not labels.
## 2) Establish Worldview Frame
How do they believe systems change?
What do they call serious? What do they dismiss as performance?
## 3) Establish Incentive Constraints
What must they protect to remain viable?
Who do they depend on? What risks are intolerable?
4) Locate the Collision

Find the recurring point where identity and worldview would imply one move, but incentives
repeatedly force another.
That collision is the contradiction.

## Pressure Confirmation Test
A contradiction is only correct if it predicts what happens under pressure.
Ask internally:
● When stakes rise, what does the donor protect first?
reputation, coherence, belonging, access, or impact?

● How do they restore safety?
abstraction, procedural control, narrowing, deferral, proxying?

If the contradiction does not predict these behaviors, it is not the real one.

How to Use Contradiction (Without Accusation)
The contradiction is not used to “call out.”
It is used to design the right invitation.
A contradiction becomes persuasive only when the meeting offers:
● a role that preserves what the donor must protect,

● while letting them act toward what they say they want.

The correct encourage ment posture is:
● structurally honest,

● low drama,

● low accusation,


● high invitation.


Contradiction as Wedge Design
A wedge is not flattery.
It is the specific alignment insight that shows:
● “you already care about this,”

● “your current posture prevents acting on it,”

● “here is a safe lane to contribute that preserves your constraints.”

The wedge solves the contradiction behaviorally, not emotionally.

## Common Failure Modes
Contradiction work fails when it becomes:
● generic (“risk vs stability”),

● purely behavioral (“says yes but delays”),

● moral (“values justice but...”),

● expressive (“torn between...”),

● canon-mimicry (reusing familiar contradiction shapes).

If it could apply to many donors, it is not correct.

## Appendix A — Contradiction Derivation Examples
(Method Only)

These examples demonstrate shape, not donor types.
They are not meant to be mapped onto donors.
They show how identity × worldview × incentives produce a primary tension that predicts
behavior.
## Example 1 — Inside Critic / Outside Constraint
Evidence: sustained critique of institutional drift + continued leadership inside institutions
requiring stability.
Contradiction: must repair from within structures whose incentives resist repair.
Meeting implication: retreat appears as abstraction or process; wedge is bounded co-design
with clear safety.
## Example 2 — Market Legitimacy / Market Fear
Evidence: finance identity + long-term giving toward civic guardrails + avoidance of ideological
conflict.
Contradiction: believes in markets yet fears what markets do without governance.
Meeting implication: prefers structural fixes; moral frames misfire; asks should be framed as
incentive repair.
## Example 3 — Movement Reliance / Movement Distrust
Evidence: capacity-building for movements + critique of mobilization cycles + cross-faction
credibility needs.
Contradiction: needs movements to succeed but distrusts their short-cycle incentives.
Meeting implication: responds to infrastructure framing; rejects slogan energy; wedge is
“durability under volatility.”
## Example 4 — Truth Stewardship / Political Vulnerability
Evidence: research institutions + integrity language + avoidance of visible activism.
Contradiction: wants truth to govern institutions but is constrained by institutions vulnerable to
capture.
Meeting implication: retreat into neutrality; wedge is distributed trust-building that stays legible
as institutional responsibility.
## Example 5 — Builder Legacy / Network Constraint
Evidence: built systems now producing second-order harms + governance focus + persistent
elite network dependence.
Contradiction: wants repair but cannot break with the networks that grant influence.
Meeting implication: needs safe lane for intervention; wedge is governance design and
distributed agency without public rupture.


## Closing Principle
The correct contradiction is the one that:
● explains pressure behavior,

● predicts retreat,

● clarifies what kind of authorship is possible,

● and makes the wedge feel structurally inevitable.

Contradiction is not drama.
It is the behavioral gateway to co-ownership.
When derived correctly, it makes the whole profile more real — and the meeting easier to win
without pitching.


Decision Reflex and Emotional Entry:
## Memo 3

## MEMO 3 — DECISION REFLEX & ENTRY
## CONDITIONS
DTW Donor Profiling Cognition Manual
## Purpose
This memo defines how to model a donor’s decision reflex:
the repeatable sequence through which a donor moves from initial engagement → internal
authorization → observable action.
A donor does not “decide” in a vacuum.
They move through a patterned progression that is:
● predictable,

● evidence-based,

● and structurally constrained by identity, worldview, and incentives.

If this reflex is not modeled correctly, Step 3 profiles feel inert and meetings misfire — not
because the idea is wrong, but because the sequence is wrong.

## Core Principle
A decision is authorized before it is expressed.
Every donor action follows this sequence:
## 1. Entry Condition
What makes engagement feel legitimate or worth attention.

## 2. Permission Logic
The internal explanation that allows the donor to act without threatening identity,
reputation, or coherence.


## 3. Behavioral Commitment
The visible form the “yes” takes (design input, introduction, funding, convening,
endorsement, delay).

This is not psychology.
It is decision mechanics.

## Why Decision Reflex Matters
Profiles fail when they:
● treat donors as static preference holders,

● assume values directly translate to action,

● design asks without sequencing authorization.

Decision reflex modeling allows you to:
● time the meeting correctly,

● avoid premature asks,

● interpret hesitation accurately,

● design roles that fit how the donor authorizes action.

Without this model, askers push at the wrong moment — and trigger retreat.

Entry Conditions (What Opens the Door)
Entry conditions are structural cues, not emotions.
They are inferred from repeated behavior indicating what must be present before a donor
engages seriously.
Common entry conditions include (forms only):

● evidence of rigor or competence,

● recognition of a real constraint,

● proximity to people they respect,

● a system failure they already worry about,

● an opportunity to be useful rather than supportive.

Entry conditions vary by donor and must be inferred from Step 2 patterns.
If the entry condition is unmet, no amount of logic will activate engagement.

Permission Logic (How the Donor Authorizes Action)
Once engaged, donors must internally justify action.
Permission logic answers the question:
“Why is it legitimate for me to do this?”
Permission logic is shaped by:
● incentive constraints,

● identity maintenance,

● reputational exposure,

● worldview coherence.

Examples of permission logic forms (not scripts):
● “This is structurally serious.”

● “This fits my role without overexposing me.”

● “This corrects a failure I already see.”

● “This is an appropriate use of my leverage.”


The donor must be able to narrate the permission to themselves — and to peers.
If permission logic is missing, the donor may remain interested but inactive.

Behavioral Commitment (What ‘Yes’ Looks Like)
The final stage is observable behavior.
Commitment may take many forms:
● critique and design input,

● quiet introductions,

● bounded experiments,

● advisory participation,

● deferred engagement,

● or financial commitment.

The key insight:
Not all “yes” actions look alike.
A correct profile predicts:
● which behaviors indicate real movement,

● which indicate deferral or retreat,

● which are early-stage but meaningful.

Misreading these signals leads to false positives or premature escalation.

Sequencing the Meeting Using Decision Reflex
Meeting choreography should follow the reflex, not override it.

Correct sequence:
- Meet the entry condition
(demonstrate seriousness, surface constraint, show coherence)

- Enable permission logic
(frame the role in a way that preserves identity and incentives)

- Offer a behaviorally appropriate commitment
(design input, test, introduction, or bounded action)

If you invert the sequence:
● logic before entry → disengagement

● affiliation before permission → reputational freeze

● commitment before authorization → retreat

The meeting fails not because the idea is weak, but because the sequence violates the donor’s
decision reflex.

## Conflicted Decision Reflexes
Some donors experience tension between entry conditions and permission logic.
Examples of conflict patterns (forms only):
● engagement requires urgency, but permission requires caution

● entry comes from recognition, but permission requires humility

● curiosity is high, but exposure tolerance is low

In these cases, the role design must:
● separate thinking from endorsing,

● allow contribution without visibility,


● create time without loss of momentum.

Profiles should model these conflicts explicitly so tactics can accommodate them.

How Decision Reflex Appears in Step 3
Decision reflex informs:
## ● Section 3 — Engagement Style
(how and when they engage)

## ● Section 6 — Tactical Approach
(how to open, pace, and close)

## ● Section 4 — Risk Factors
(what triggers withdrawal)

## ● Section 7 — Dinner Test
(how informal engagement signals permission)

Decision reflex should never appear as a label or theory in the profile.
It must be embedded behaviorally.

## Common Failure Modes
Decision reflex modeling fails when profiles:
● list motivations without sequence,

● describe emotions without authorization logic,

● treat all “interest” as equivalent,

● assume funding is the default outcome,

● confuse enthusiasm with permission.


These errors produce polite meetings with no movement.

## Internal Accuracy Test
Before finalizing decision reflex modeling, ask:
● Can I predict what must happen before this donor acts?

● Can I explain why they would be allowed to act without identity cost?

● Can I distinguish interest from authorization?

● Can I name the smallest meaningful commitment they could make?

If any answer is no, the reflex is under-modeled.

## Closing Principle
A donor’s “yes” is not persuasion.
It is authorization.
Profiles that model decision reflex correctly allow askers to:
● stop pushing,

● start sequencing,

● and design roles that donors can actually inhabit.

That is the difference between a good idea and a usable profile.


## Chemistry Mapping: Memo 4

## MEMO 4 — CHEMISTRY MAPPING
## (RELATIONAL MECHANICS)
DTW Donor Profiling Cognition Manual
## Purpose
This memo teaches how to model donor–asker chemistry as a function of power, posture, and
perception — not personality or “fit.”
Chemistry is not interpersonal warmth.
It is the donor’s structural reading of the asker under conditions of uncertainty, authority, and
risk.
A persuasion profile fails if it describes the donor accurately but leaves the asker behaving in a
way the donor cannot metabolize.
This memo ensures profiles do not just describe donors — they design the asker’s posture.

## Core Principle
Donors do not hear proposals before they classify the asker.
Within moments, the donor forms a working assessment of:
● what role the asker occupies,

● how much authority they hold,

● whether they understand power,

● whether they are safe, useful, or costly to engage.

Everything that follows — openness, skepticism, distance, generosity — flows from that
assessment.
Chemistry mapping models this classification process.


## What Chemistry Mapping Actually Is
Chemistry mapping is:
● a predictive model of how the donor will read the asker,

● a counterfactual simulation of what will go wrong if posture is misaligned,

● a behavioral calibration tool for tone, sequencing, and invitation.

It is not style matching.
It is not rapport building.
It is not likability engineering.
It is relational mechanics under power.

What the Donor Is Evaluating
Across donors, the evaluation is remarkably consistent in structure, even as content differs.
The donor is silently asking:
● Does this person understand systems the way I do?

● Do they respect constraints — including mine?

● Are they trying to persuade me, or think with me?

● Will engagement increase my agency or cost it?

Chemistry is the donor’s answer to these questions.

Inputs to Chemistry Mapping
To model chemistry correctly, you must consider both sides:

Donor-Side Inputs
Derived from prior cognition modules:
● worldview engine

● incentives and exposure tolerance

● presence and retreat patterns

● decision reflex

● systems fear

Asker-Side Inputs
Provided implicitly or explicitly:
● institutional role and authority

● proximity to power

● fluency with donor’s domain

● default posture (pitcher, builder, operator, advocate)

● emotional register under pressure

Chemistry emerges from how these interact, not from either alone.

Common Chemistry Failure Patterns (Structural, Not
## Moral)
Chemistry breaks in predictable ways:
## Posture Compression
The asker minimizes themselves (supplicant, student, moral witness).
Result: donor remains polite but disengages; authority imbalance widens.

## Posture Inflation
The asker overclaims (visionary, future-builder, moral authority).
Result: donor feels displaced or managed; skepticism rises.
## Register Mismatch
The asker leads with story when donor needs structure — or vice versa.
Result: donor never reaches authorization stage.
## Constraint Blindness
The asker ignores incentive or exposure limits.
Result: donor retreats to safety language or abstraction.
These are not personality conflicts.
They are misaligned role assumptions.

How to Run the Chemistry Simulation (Internal Algorithm)
Before finalizing a profile, simulate this sequence:
## 1. Initial Classification
How does the donor likely categorize the asker within the first minute?
## Operator? Advocate? Builder? Intermediary? Risk?

- Threat or Utility Scan
Does the asker increase clarity or cost?
Do they introduce noise or sharpen thinking?

## 3. Authority Calibration
Is authority shared, ceded, or contested?
Is the asker legible as a peer, junior, or distraction?

## 4. Engagement Decision
Does the donor lean in, test, abstract, or delay?

If you cannot answer these steps, the profile’s tactical sections will fail.


What Chemistry Mapping Must Produce in Step 3
Correct chemistry mapping appears implicitly, not as a labeled section.
It should shape:
● Section 1 — how the donor will read the asker

● Section 3 — engagement style and testing behavior

● Section 6 — meeting choreography and recovery moves

● Section 7 — informal cognition and social calibration

Chemistry should be felt as guidance, not declared as theory.

Designing the Asker’s Posture (Without Coaching
## Language)
A correct profile causes the asker to:
● stop justifying the work,

● stop performing conviction,

● stop chasing approval,

● and start offering structure, constraint, or unfinished thinking.

This happens because the profile repositions the asker’s role in relation to the donor.
If the profile does not cause this shift, chemistry was not modeled deeply enough.

Observable Signals of Chemistry Alignment
When chemistry is correct, donors typically:

● test ideas rather than dismiss them,

● sharpen language instead of reframing it away,

● ask about constraints rather than outcomes,

● offer critique without retreating,

● suggest next steps that preserve their agency.

When chemistry is off, donors typically:
● stay warm but abstract,

● redirect to others,

● slow the timeline,

● emphasize values over mechanics,

● exit without conflict.

Profiles must allow askers to distinguish these states accurately.

Common Modeling Errors to Avoid
Chemistry mapping fails when the model:
● relies on archetypes (“this donor likes builders”),

● imagines internal monologues,

● describes feelings rather than evaluation mechanics,

● treats chemistry as mutual rather than donor-driven,

● offers generic advice (“be authentic,” “show humility”).

None of these change behavior in the room.


## Internal Accuracy Test
Before approving chemistry mapping, ask:
● Would this change how the asker opens the meeting?

● Would it change what they don’t say?

● Would it change how they interpret donor silence or skepticism?

● Would it prevent at least one predictable misstep?

If not, chemistry has not been modeled.

## Closing Principle
A donor profile must describe the donor accurately.
A DTW profile must also design the asker’s behavior.
Chemistry mapping is where persuasion becomes relationally possible.
When done correctly, the donor experiences the asker as:
“Someone who understands how to think with me.”
That is the precondition for every meaningful yes.


## Asshole Mode Diagnostics: Memo 5

## MEMO 5 — DEFENSIVE POSTURE
## DIAGNOSTICS (“ASSHOLE MODE”)
DTW Donor Profiling Cognition Manual
## Purpose
This memo teaches how to detect, model, and recover from a donor’s defensive posture
under threat — what this system has called “asshole mode.”
This is not personality typing.
It is operational pattern recognition.
Asshole mode is:
● situational,

● predictable,

● and almost always triggered by a specific kind of threat to coherence, identity, or
incentive safety.

If you can model it correctly, profiles:
● prevent rupture,

● reduce misreads,

● and equip askers to recover quickly when the meeting starts to tilt.


## Core Principle
Everyone has a defense ritual.
The job is not to avoid donors’ defenses through deference.
The job is to:

● understand what the defense is protecting,

● avoid triggering it unnecessarily,

● and recover fast if it appears.

Asshole mode is rarely “aggression.”
It is often signal management: a way to control noise, protect status, or preserve optionality
when the donor feels exposed.

## What Asshole Mode Protects
In practice, asshole mode is usually protecting one of four things:
- Coherence — “This doesn’t hold.”

- Time — “I’m not spending cycles on this.”

- Reputation / Exposure — “Don’t put me near something sloppy or risky.”

- Relevance — “I still set the bar here.”

Your task is to infer which protection function is active.

## How Asshole Mode Appears
Asshole mode can be loud or quiet.
It often appears as a shift in meeting mechanics, not a change in stated opinion.
Common signals include:
● tempo changes (faster interrogation or slower proceduralism),

● tightening scope (narrowing to definitions or edge cases),

● movement into process language,


● increased formality,

● sarcasm or clipped precision,

● warm politeness with cognitive absence,

● abrupt handoff to proxies.

The key is not the behavior itself.
It is the direction of the shift.

Reading Asshole Mode as a Signal, Not a Verdict
When defensive posture appears, do not interpret it as:
● disinterest,

● hostility,

● or a permanent no.

Interpret it as:
“Something just became unsafe, incoherent, or costly.”
The question becomes:
unsafe for what?

Common Defensive Motions (Illustrative Forms)
These are motion patterns, not donor categories.
A donor can exhibit more than one.
## Precision Clamp
They lock onto definitions, numbers, logic chain integrity.

● Usually protects: coherence or status.

● Correct response: honor the precision; invite a bounded stress test rather than rebuttal.

## Procedural Cushion
They shift into process: “loop in X,” “send a memo,” “let’s revisit.”
● Usually protects: exposure or time.

● Correct response: shrink the ask to a low-pressure diagnostic contribution.

## Frame Takeover
They start rewriting your model mid-meeting.
● Usually protects: reputational risk (“I won’t be near something sloppy”).

● Correct response: convert takeover into a structured critique role; define boundaries of
authorship.

## Warm Detachment
They remain pleasant but stop building.
● Usually protects: optionality and social smoothness.

● Correct response: introduce a crisp constraint or tradeoff; ask one bounded question
that requires a fingerprint.


Prevention: How Not to Trigger Defensive Posture
Defensive posture is often triggered by predictable meeting errors:
- Leading with shine
Polished narratives and self-congratulation read as spin.
Start with what is brittle, contested, or not yet solved.

- Asking for validation
“Do you agree?” invites status defense.
Instead ask: “Where does this break?” “What’s the failure mode?”
- Mis-sizing exposure
Public ask too early triggers reputational safety moves.
Offer an entry lane that matches their exposure metabolism.
- Over-claiming certainty
Certainty without constraint reads as unserious.
Name the limits early.
Prevention is not deference.
It is correct calibration.

Recovery: What to Do When Asshole Mode Appears
When defensive posture emerges:
- Do not rebut.
Rebuttal escalates threat.

- Shift to shared inquiry.
Ask a question that invites co-diagnosis rather than debate.

- Shrink the commitment ask.
Move from endorsement to critique, from public to private, from big to bounded.

- Name the seam, not the emotion.
Return to the unresolved constraint.

A reliable reset move is:
“That’s helpful. Let’s isolate the brittle part and test it.”
Recovery is posture correction, not persuasion effort.


How to Render This in Step 3
Profiles should:
● describe the donor’s likely defensive motion,

● name what tends to trigger it,

● show what it looks like in the room,

● and provide one recovery move that keeps the meeting alive.

This is not to “neutralize” the donor.
It is to prevent misreads and preserve productive engagement.

Integration with Other Memos
Asshole mode is rarely random.
It is usually produced by the interaction of:
● Substrate reconstruction (what they protect)

● Contradiction (where they are exposed)

● Decision reflex (what sequence they require)

● Chemistry mapping (what role they assign the asker)

● Presence/retreat patterns (how they express discomfort)

Defensive posture is often the visible surface of a deeper mismatch.

Before writing asshole-mode guidance, ask:
● Is this derived from repeated behavior or pressure evidence?


● Does it identify what is being protected?

● Does it change how the asker behaves?

● Does it avoid moral judgment or caricature?

If yes, it belongs.
If no, it is projection.

## Closing Principle
Asshole mode is not aggression.
It is a donor’s way of keeping coherence, safety, and signal intact under pressure.
Profiles that model it correctly make meetings harder to lose.
The goal is not avoidance.
The goal is preparedness:
know what breaks, know what it protects, and know how to restore alignment quickly.

● what they can risk (incentives),

● what they fear will break (systems fear),

Fight vs Build (Functional Definitions)

## Fight Orientation
Fight is the donor’s preference for:
● confrontation as correction,

● pressure as signal,

● conflict as a mechanism to expose misalignment,

● forcing decisions through contested moments.

Fight-oriented donors often treat conflict as an information tool.
## Build Orientation
Build is the donor’s preference for:
● constructing durable structures,

● convening or standard-setting,

● governance and capacity development,

● creating systems that outlast a cycle.


Build-oriented donors often treat construction as the real form of power.
## Hybrid Orientation
Many donors are hybrids:
● fight to reveal constraints, then build to stabilize,

● build first, then fight to defend or scale,

● oscillate depending on where incentives and risk sit in a given moment.

The goal is correct sequencing.

● what kinds of organizations they fund or build,

● what kinds of wins they celebrate,

● what kinds of language they trust (pressure vs architecture),

● what kind of conflict they enter or avoid,

● whether they prefer exposure or insulation,

● how they behave when disagreement appears.

Do not infer from stated values.
Infer from repeated investment in mechanisms.

Why This Matters for Meeting Design
Fight/build posture shapes:

● how to open the meeting,

● what kind of tension to surface,

● what kind of role invitation lands,

● what “progress” looks like in their head,

● what kind of follow-up keeps them engaged.

Misalignment looks like:
● donors going abstract when you go confrontational,

● donors going procedural when you go urgent,

● donors offering ideas but resisting ownership when you ask for co-signing,

● donors shifting to safety language when you ask for exposure.

These are posture signals, not disinterest.

Leadership Arc (Where They Locate Themselves in the
## Work)

It is how they locate themselves in responsibility.

Collective-anchored posture

Opportunity: bounded experiments with clear escalation triggers.

## 2. Role

## 3. Exposure

A correct ask is one that feels legitimate to their posture.

This should not appear as labels.
It should appear as usable choreography.

● generic (“they like urgency”),

● grid-driven (“place them in a box”),

● detached from incentives and exposure constraints.

Fight/build is not temperament.
It is an interaction between architecture and context.

## Core Principle

It means the donor can see a role that:
● fits their identity posture,

● respects their incentives,

● uses their real competence,

## What Authorship Solves
Correct authorship design:
● prevents meetings from becoming feedback theater,

● converts critique into contribution,

● creates a second conversation,

● makes the donor’s “yes” durable rather than symbolic.

Incorrect authorship design produces:
● polite validation with no fingerprints,

● hyper-help without commitment,

● proxy passing,

● do they critique?

● do they edit?

● do they add a constraint?

● do they propose a next iteration?

● do they offer a name or connection?

The moment they start shaping, authorship becomes possible.

3) History and Role Pattern

● builder roles → architecture and refinement

● long-tenure roles → stability holding

● network roles → curation and acceleration

● governance roles → guardrails and legitimacy

Do not stereotype by profession.
Infer from repeated behavior patterns.

## 4) Retreat Style

● Refinement-oriented donors disengage when logic is sloppy.

● Curation-oriented donors disengage when the social pattern is already locked.

● Stability-oriented donors disengage when risk is unmanaged.

Retreat is often the clearest indicator of what authorship requires.

- Explain urgency without drama

- Clarify what counts as “serious” work
What kinds of action feel legitimate to them.

- Create alignment wedge
Why DTW’s work is a credible mechanism inside their system model.

How to Derive Dangerous Truth Safely

## 1. System Focus
What system do they believe is failing?

## 2. Failure Mechanism
Why do they believe failure is occurring?
(drift, capture, incentive corruption, britt tdleness, fragmentation)

- Default Non-Repair
Why do they believe the system will not self-correct?
(incentive lock-in, reputational denial, weak governance, missing distributed agency)

## 4. Intervention Theory
What kind of action do they believe changes the outcome?

● what they refuse to engage (diagnostic absence),

● what they fund even when it is unpopular or hard.

You are looking for recurrence, not quotability.

A dangerous truth must be stated in:
● plain, structural language,

● civic-scale terms,

● incentive-aware framing,

● calm tone.

It should sound:
● unavoidable,

● not performative.

● “She believes institutions will continue drifting because they cannot admit failure quickly
enough to adapt.”

● “He believes markets will keep rewarding harm unless new governance layers change
incentive behavior.”

● “She believes distributed agency is missing, and without it no system can correct itself
under shock.”

If the sentence feels dramatic, it is probably wrong.
If it feels structurally obvious, it is probably right.

● reputational gossip (“they can’t be seen...” without evidence),

● Is this civic-scale rather than personal?

Self/Us/Now Sequencing: Memo 10

## MEMO 10 — SELF / US / NOW
## SEQUENCING (TEMPORAL DECISION
## LOGIC)
DTW Donor Profiling Cognition Manual
## Purpose
This memo teaches how to infer and apply a donor’s temporal decision posture — whether
they are operating primarily in a Self, Us, or Now frame — and how that posture governs:
● how they authorize action,

● what kind of authorship feels legitimate,

● how they interpret risk and urgency,

● why certain asks feel mistimed even when aligned,

● and how persuasion collapses when timing is wrong.

Self / Us / Now is not narrative.
It is time-based authorization logic.
Donors do not decide what to do first.
They decide when they are allowed to act — and in what role.

## Core Principle
Every donor evaluates an ask through a temporal permission filter.
That filter answers, often unconsciously:
● Is this something I should act on as an individual?


● Is this something that requires collective alignment first?

● Is this something that must happen now or not at all?

If the ask is framed in the wrong temporal register:
● alignment feels performative,

● urgency feels manipulative,

● authorship feels inappropriate,

● and retreat follows quickly.

Correct sequencing does not persuade the donor.
It allows the donor to persuade themselves.

The Three Temporal Modes (Functional Definitions)
SELF — Individual Authorization Mode
Self mode governs moments where the donor’s identity, judgment, or responsibility is central.
Operational characteristics:
● evaluates alignment through personal credibility and experience

● authorizes action through individual judgment

● prefers bounded authorship over shared ownership

● sensitive to misrecognition or bypassing of experience

Behavioral indicators:
● references to prior work, lessons learned, scars, or responsibility

● desire to shape logic, constraints, or standards directly


● discomfort with being treated as interchangeable with peers

Failure mode if misread:
Donor disengages if treated as a node rather than a decision-maker.

US — Collective Authorization Mode
Us mode governs moments where legitimacy must be shared or buffered through peers.
Operational characteristics:
● evaluates alignment through field coherence and peer signals

● authorizes action through collective readiness

● prefers convening, curating, or shaping the room

● avoids unilateral exposure

Behavioral indicators:
● frequent reference to “the field,” “peers,” or adjacent actors

● questions about who else is involved

● interest in shaping criteria rather than making the decision

Failure mode if misread:
Donor freezes if asked to act alone or too visibly.

NOW — Temporal Window Mode
Now mode governs moments when the donor perceives a closing window.
Operational characteristics:
● evaluates alignment through consequence of delay


● authorizes action through time-bound logic

● prefers scoped, reversible, or catalytic moves

● tolerates risk only when time logic is clear

Behavioral indicators:
● compression language (“this cycle,” “before X happens”)

● impatience with long onboarding or abstraction

● interest in first moves rather than full plans

Failure mode if misread:
Donor exits if the ask feels slow, diffuse, or long-horizon.

Arc Detection (How to Infer the Active Mode)
Temporal mode is inferred from behavioral evidence, not stated preference.
Primary evidence sources
● language patterns over time

● pacing of projects and commitments

● convening behavior (who, when, how)

● tolerance for exposure and ambiguity

● retreat behavior when urgency or ownership appears

Key diagnostic question
What kind of action does this donor currently allow themselves to take without
identity threat?
The answer reveals the active mode.


Arc Transitions (Why Timing Matters More Than Fit)
Donors move between modes predictably.
Common transitions:
● Self → Us: after personal credibility is stabilized

● Us → Now: when coordination stalls or risk externalizes

● Now → Self: after burnout or urgency collapse

Profiles must:
● identify the current mode,

● note the mode being exited,

● anticipate the next mode likely to open.

Persuasion fails most often at transition points.

Using Self / Us / Now in Step-3 Profiles
Where it belongs
● Identity & Background (how they authorize themselves)

● Engagement Style (how they permit interaction)

● Strategic Opportunities (what kind of role fits now)

● Tactical Approach (how to time and frame the ask)

How it should appear

Never labeled.
Never explained explicitly.
Always embedded in behavior, posture, and invitation logic.
Example (form only):
“Currently avoids unilateral commitments; responds when peer alignment is visible.
Treat as a shaper of criteria, not a first mover.”

## Temporal Misalignment: Predictable Failure Modes
## Donor Mode Misframed Ask Result

## Advanced Use: Designing Safe Transitions
Effective asks often bridge modes rather than lock into one.
Examples (form only):
● Self → Us: move from design authorship to convening role

● Us → Now: convert consensus into a bounded first action

● Now → Self: offer post-action stewardship or review

Profiles should signal where these bridges exist — without forcing them.

## Integration With Other Memos

● Would reframing the ask into another mode cause friction or retreat?

● Does the profile prevent mistimed urgency or misplaced authorship?

If no → the profile will feel “off” even if accurate.

## Calibration Testing: Memo 11

## MEMO 11 — CALIBRATION TESTING
## (PROFILE PERFORMANCE
## VERIFICATION)
DTW Donor Profiling Cognition Manual
## Purpose
This memo defines how to evaluate whether a donor persuasion profile functions under real
conditions.
Calibration is not editorial review.
Calibration is performance verification.
A profile is correct only if it:
● changes how the asker behaves,

● changes how the meeting is designed,

● changes how the donor engages,

● and produces clearer next actions than would have occurred without it.

If none of those happen, the profile has failed — regardless of how accurate, elegant, or
psychologically insightful it appears.

## Core Principle
Profiles are not validated by agreement.
They are validated by changed behavior.
Calibration asks one question:
Did this profile alter real decisions, sequencing, posture, or design — in ways that
increased traction?

If the answer is no, the profile is not complete.

## What Calibration Is (and Is Not)
Calibration IS
● a test of field utility

● a check for behavioral consequence

● a way to detect flattening, drift, or misalignment

● a requirement for Step-4 elevation

Calibration is NOT
● whether the profile “reads well”

● whether the donor compliments the framing

● whether the team feels affirmed

● whether the analysis feels deep

A profile that feels good but does nothing is not calibrated.

## The Three Calibration Axes
Every profile must perform across three independent axes.
## 1. Behavioral Calibration
Did people act differently because of the profile?
## Indicators:
● Asker posture changed (tone, pacing, confidence, restraint)


● Meeting design changed (who attended, what was shown, what was withheld)

● The donor engaged differently than they typically do

If no one adjusted behavior, the profile failed this axis.

## 2. Temporal Calibration
Did the profile hold across time and phases?
## Indicators:
● It shaped pre-meeting preparation

● It held during live interaction

● It clarified post-meeting continuity

Profiles that only “work in the room” but collapse afterward are incomplete.

## 3. Transfer Calibration
Can the profile travel without its author?
## Indicators:
● Another team member can use it without reinterpretation

● It works across formats (memo, Zoom, dinner, email)

● It predicts friction before it appears

If a profile only works when explained by its author, it is not calibrated.

Pre-Meeting Calibration Checks

Before a profile is used, it must force at least one concrete change.
## 1. Meeting Design Shift
## Ask:
● Was the sequence altered?

● Was the opening changed?

● Was the attendee list adjusted?

If the meeting plan is identical to the default plan, the profile has not done work.

## 2. Ask Rescoping
## Ask:
● Was the authorship role resized?

● Was the exposure level adjusted?

● Was the timing reframed?

If the ask is unchanged, the profile has not engaged incentive logic.

## 3. Language Discipline
## Ask:
● Were opening lines rewritten?

● Were banned vocabularies removed?

● Was the frame made more structural?

If scripts stayed the same, the profile is ornamental.


Live-Meeting Calibration Signals
During the meeting, calibrated profiles predict specific inflection points.
Primary indicators
● The donor interrupts to reframe or sharpen

● The donor edits logic, not tone

● The donor asks a question that changes direction

● The donor introduces a constraint or test

These are signals of authorship activation.

Negative indicators
● Polite agreement with no structural engagement

● Values affirmation replacing design interaction

● Abstract praise with no follow-up questions

These indicate miscalibration.

Post-Meeting Calibration Tests
After the meeting, calibration shows up in continuity behavior.
- Follow-Up Precision
## Ask:
● Was the follow-up artifact matched to authorship mode?

● Did the donor respond in kind (edit, critique, extension)?


Generic follow-ups signal profile failure.

## 2. Role Emergence
## Ask:
● Did a next role appear naturally?

● Did the donor suggest names, language, or structure?

If the donor disappears or defers indefinitely, revisit calibration.

## 3. Handoff Viability
## Ask:
● Can another person carry the thread forward?

● Does the profile still guide behavior without explanation?

Profiles that require constant translation are not field-ready.

change

Calibration Questions (Internal Use)
Before deployment:
● Would this profile change the meeting if no deck were allowed?

● Does it expose something the donor already feels but hasn’t named?

After deployment:
● What happened that would not have happened without this profile?

● Where did the donor deviate from their usual pattern — as predicted?

Inter-Memo Integration
Calibration verifies whether other memos are functioning:
● Contradiction: Did it shape risk or opportunity?

● Authorship: Did the donor step into a role?

● Presence / Retreat: Did predicted behaviors appear?

## Common Calibration Failures
● Profiles that are “accurate” but inert


● Profiles that sound donor-specific but don’t guide action

● Profiles that predict behavior but don’t shape it

● Profiles that collapse under replication

All are failures.

## Calibration Is Not Punitive
Calibration does not judge intelligence or effort.
It evaluates fitness for use.
Profiles that fail calibration are not wrong — they are incomplete.
They require:
● sharper contradiction

● clearer incentive modeling

● better temporal sequencing

● or removal of generic language


## Canon Reminder
A donor profile is not a description.
It is a rehearsal for a meeting that hasn’t happened yet.
If it doesn’t rehearse behavior —
if it doesn’t change posture —
if it doesn’t make the next move clearer —
