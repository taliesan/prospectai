Every donor lives inside a self‑story.
Not a brand, not a resume blurb, not a LinkedIn headline — a narrative logic about who they
are, what they build, how they matter, and what kind of world they believe they are helping
create.
This self‑story is a form of power.
It shapes how they use their money, their reputation, their relationships, their attention, and
their influence. It determines what they see, what they miss, what they fear, what they avoid,
what they respond to, and what they interpret as meaningfully “theirs.”
A donor profile that does not capture a donor’s self‑story is incomplete.
It may be accurate, but it will not be persuasive.
A self‑story is not fiction.
It is the composite of a donor’s choices, tone, values, patterns, affiliations, ambitions, regrets,
contradictions, and absences.
It is the story they tell unconsciously in how they move through the world.
Self‑story answers the deeper questions beneath all behavior:
● Who do they think they are?
● What do they believe they are for?
● What arc do they imagine themselves on?
● What role do they want to play in history, or in their field, or in their community?
● What version of themselves are they trying to become?
● What version are they trying to escape?
● Where do they feel responsibility, guilt, inherited obligation, or destiny?
● Where do they feel they haven’t yet delivered on their potential?
● What story collapses their defenses and makes them feel recognized instead of
managed?
Self‑story is the part of a donor that cannot be reached with pitch decks, bullet points, or rational
appeals alone.
It is the part that responds to recognition — the feeling of being understood not as a
checkbook, but as a human being with an identity and a narrative arc.
In persuasion, self‑story is where emotion lives.
And emotion is what precedes a donor’s “yes.”
```
Therefore:
```
Profiles may — and should — articulate a donor’s self‑story in clear, compressed,
psychologically true language when supported by observable patterns. This is not flattery. It is
not projection. It is not mind‑reading. It is strategic clarity.

Self‑story is a persuasion surface because:
● It clarifies what makes a donor feel seen.

● It reveals the psychological “slot” where your work fits.
● It exposes the contradictions that create unstable openings for persuasion.
● It aligns the work with the donor’s internal narrative so the “yes” feels like their idea, not
yours.
Self‑story does not replace the power ledger, ego analysis, or strategic alignment.
It is the emotional substrate that makes those tools usable.
Understanding a donor’s self‑story is how we protect the work, avoid supplicancy, preserve
authorship, and build durable co‑creative partnerships.
Profiles exist to reveal the self‑story beneath the surface narrative.
This is how persuasion becomes not performance, but clarity.

You’ve described the bottom half of a LinkedIn profile as the place where the mask slips —
where curated narrative gives way to pattern, psychology, and subtext.
```
It’s where the story of what drives someone (and how they see themselves) is visible between
```

```

1. Narrative Continuity vs. Discontinuity

These often mark moments of crisis, reinvention, or conflict between values and
ambition.

alignment.

```
they were successful.

● Look for the story behind the school — not prestige, but tribal belonging:

● Fight - Advantages
○ Simple. They are boiled down, refined. Often to a binary choice. X or Y. Us or
them. They are free from confusion and complication. They cut through bullshit.
Make things clear.
○ Grounded in the now. They are often connected to events that are very present,
unfolding in real time. They are immediate and understood. An election.
Responding to a disaster. Fighting off a threat. Defeating or passing proposed
legislation.
○ They are accessible. People who aren’t part of your base or usual community
can understand a fight narrative. They know what’s at stake, how it matters to
them, and what they need to do. They are easy to join.

should/shouldn’t be included, etc., tend to fall away in face of the realities and
priorities of the moment. It’s easier to mobilize around a fight narrative.
○ There is a villain. A clear opponent that can be called out and demonized. There
is a cause that can be isolated and removed.
○ Often–there’s a moment it’s over. You know a decision is being made. So you
can go all in on the energy expenditure, knowing that you’ll be able to rest the
day after, win or lose.
● Fight - Disadvantages
○ They’re divisive. While there’s unity on your side, you’re dividing the larger
population into camps. You’re creating and identifying an opponent, someone to
blame. They are ideological and tribal.
○ There’s no room for nuance. Their clarity and urgency means folks need to fall in
line behind a simple choice, a single action. There’s no room for exploration,
uncertainty, or complication.
○ They consume energy. Fight narratives are sugar highs. They do massive
amounts of work in a very short window. But they dwindle very quickly. They are
next to impossible to sustain over the long term without frying your base.
○ As a result of the energy consumption, they tend to be short lived. Orgs that yell
‘fight, fight, fight’ start to feel juvenile, annoying. There’s a moment to fight and
it’s not all day every day. That’s not sustainable.

● It’s not quite as simple as the opposites of all those. Though it’s close.
● Build - Advantages
○ They’re inclusive and uniting. While fight narratives join your existing folks
together behind a single objective, build narratives have space to welcome new
points of view, to educate and engage, to adapt. To reach out to new camps,
broaden the coalition.
○ There’s room for nuance, complication, and uncertainty. You have the time and
space to figure things out. To deal with new and conflicting facts. To look at the
larger picture and all its connections.
8
○ They’re slower. Meaning there’s time. It doesn’t have to be an all-out sprint. You
can work on it within a larger time frame.
○ They give energy. A fight narrative takes your energy. A build narrative can give
you energy, hope, community. It can sustain you over time.
○ It’s unbounded. Fight narratives are focused on a binary choice. Build narratives
can contain an unlimited and exciting number of choices, outcomes, potential.
○ They can be your identity. Your life’s work and purpose.
● Build - Disadvantages
○ They’re slower. While you have time, people can get impatient. They can drift,
wanting more action, something to happen, measurable progress in the short
term. They can be worried the other side is advancing faster.
○ The timeline can be too long. Too much work before we see something real. Too
much trust required that, eventually, this will all pay off.
○ They can be tedious. Circular. Endlessly multivariable. Too expansive. Dominated
by wonks who won’t let things go.
○ And the biggest is that they lack urgency when competing with a fight. People will
always prioritize a fight. ‘Yes, clearly this matters. I just need to deal with this
other raging fire and then I can focus.’ But there’s always going to be another fire.
A build narrative will always start tomorrow. It’s very hard to get it started today.
You have to first win the fight in front of you.
