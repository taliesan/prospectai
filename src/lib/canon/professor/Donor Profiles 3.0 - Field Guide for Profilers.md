

## Rendering Layer — Introduction

Rendering Layer — Introduction (Updated
## Canon)
The Field Guide governs not only how profilers understand donors, but how that understanding
must be rendered on the page. The Rendering Layer ensures that every persuasion profile
expresses the DTW signature: behavioral clarity, psychological depth, and immediate
usefulness in real conversations.
A donor persuasion profile is not an essay, a narrative arc, or a personality summary.
It is a sequence of behavioral insights — each one:
● grounded in how the donor actually moves under pressure,

● specific enough to alter the asker’s approach,

● anchored in interiority and incentive structures,

● written so that a skilled fundraiser can act on it immediately.

Form is part of the persuasion logic.
The structure forces clarity: each bullet captures an essential behavioral truth; each section
shows how that truth manifests in real, high-stakes interaction.
To maintain coherence and scan-speed across all Step-3 profiles, the surface grammar remains
constant:
● 7 canonical sections, always in the same order

● Bullets only — no paragraphs, exposition, or category labels

● Bullets written as observed or reliably inferred behavior, not traits

● Conditional logic embedded in each line (“when...”, “if...”, “under pressure...”)

● Each bullet contains an implication for how a skilled asker should interpret or respond

● Interiority and strategy fused into every behavioral description

When done correctly, Step-3 profiles feel:

● intimate without sentimentality,

● psychologically revealing while tactically grounded,

● shaped uniquely to the donor rather than to a template,

● and immediately usable in the first 10 seconds of a real meeting.

The Rendering Layer is what turns the rest of this guide — contradictions, tells, presence
patterns, ego structure, agency instincts, hinge moments, rupture cues — into a finished
persuasion instrument.
This section defines the standards that instrument must meet.


## Field Ergonomics Specification

Field Ergonomics Specification (Updated
## Canon)
Part of the Field Guide Series
## Purpose
Define the layout, clarity standards, and sentence mechanics required for persuasion profiles to
function in real donor interactions — live meetings, asynchronous review, hallway consults, and
high-pressure moments.
The Field Ergonomics Specification governs the mechanical intelligence that makes profiles
scannable, quotable, psychologically alive, and immediately useful to someone walking into a
consequential conversation.

## Canon Doctrine
“Profiles aren’t read. They’re used.
If a profile can’t shape a room in 30 seconds, it isn’t ergonomic.”
Ergonomics = structural clarity + tonal precision + scan-speed utility.
A field-ready profile is:
● built for speed, not flourish,

● formatted for in-flight use (phone screen, Zoom window, side-room consult),

● designed to operate at a glance, in silence, or under emotional pressure.

A functional profile enables the asker to:
● scan the entire document in under three minutes,

● grasp the donor’s posture in thirty seconds,

● capture one orienting insight in ten seconds that reorients the pitch.



Layout Architecture (Canonical Surface Grammar)
Every persuasion profile uses the same seven canonical sections, in a fixed order:
## 1. Donor Identity & Background

## 2. Core Motivations, Values & Triggers

## 3. Ideal Engagement Style

## 4. Challenges & Risk Factors

- Strategic Opportunities for Alignment

- Tactical Approach to the Meeting

## 7. Dinner Party Test

The architecture is constant.
The density is not.
Each section reveals a distinct facet of the donor’s behavior surface.
Sections must not repeat one another; each offers new leverage.
Bullets are the atomic unit of persuasion.
They are written as observed or reliably inferred behavior, never traits.
They must contain conditional logic (“when...”, “if...”, “under pressure...”) and an implied
consequence for how a skilled asker should act or interpret the moment.
There is no fixed bullet count per section.
Use as many bullets as needed to represent the donor’s behavioral landscape — no more, no
less.
Completeness matters more than symmetry.

Bullet Logic (Functional Requirements)
A persuasion bullet must:

● reveal a behavioral pattern, not a preference,

● describe how that behavior shifts under pressure,

● encode a trigger or condition,

● imply a tactical consequence for the asker,

● fuse interiority and strategy into a single micro-insight.

Bullets are concise but not skeletal.
Two to five sentences is typical, but density is determined by necessity, not rule.
Bullets should interleave:
● structural insight (how power, incentives, or systems shape them),

● psychological tells (defensiveness, curiosity, volatility, withholding),

● behavioral predictions (what they do when the stakes rise),

● tactical guidance (how the asker should calibrate tone, timing, or posture).

Contradiction is a core ergonomic principle.
Whenever the donor’s stated values diverge from their in-room behavior, name the hinge:
● “Says he values candor — but retreats when disagreement appears without framing.”

● “Invites co-design — until authorship implies shared risk.”

These are not rhetorical flourishes; they teach the asker what to watch for.

Sentence Mechanics (Operational Rules)
The voice must be:
● direct, behavioral, active,

● grounded in observable motion,


● rigorous without ornament,

● free of hedging unless hedging is itself diagnostic (“If pressed, she may...”),

● metaphorical only when the metaphor is a clarifying diagnostic tool.

The functional shape of a bullet is:
behavior → friction → turn → implication
Example patterns (genericized):
● “Engages quickly when the problem is framed clearly — but slows the moment intent
becomes vague, signaling the need for firmer grounding.”

● “Is enthusiastic about bold ideas — until ownership is ambiguous, at which point she
becomes cautious, signaling a need to define roles explicitly.”

● “Responds well to narrative — unless the narrative glosses over tension, prompting her
to reintroduce the unresolved contradiction.”

These lines are operational, not aesthetic.
They change how the asker shows up.

Section Functions (Expanded Canon)
## 1. Donor Identity & Background
Reveal the donor’s formation: institutional imprint, class or geography, formative ruptures,
failures, mentors, political inheritance.
Outcome: The asker can model the donor’s posture in three sentences.
## 2. Core Motivations, Values & Triggers
Clarify what frames their yes/no: worldview, incentives, ideological tension, personal stakes,
central contradiction.
Outcome: The asker can anticipate their emotional pivot under pressure.
## 3. Ideal Engagement Style

Describe how to enter the donor’s cognitive channel: tone, pacing, critique bandwidth, co-design
tolerance, authorship fluency.
Outcome: The asker knows how to show up without breaking chemistry.
## 4. Challenges & Risk Factors
Surface rupture risks: ego shape, ghost patterns, authorship fears, narrative allergies,
defensiveness patterns.
Outcome: The asker avoids the three most likely meeting failures.
- Strategic Opportunities for Alignment
Translate psychology into leverage: openings, authorship lanes, contradiction-based inflection
points.
Outcome: The strategist knows which doors the donor will open — and under what conditions.
- Tactical Approach to the Meeting
Provide the emotional opener, the mid-pitch pivot, the authorship trigger, and the follow-up path.
Outcome: The asker can run the entire conversation from this section alone.
## 7. Dinner Party Test
Model unmoderated cognition: social physics, curiosity, boredom thresholds, trust cues,
conversational red flags.
Outcome: The asker avoids tone errors in informal but politically meaningful rooms.

## Canon Rhythm
Profiles should carry an implicit energy arc — not as a rule, but as a functional pattern:
## Reverence → Reveal → Tension → Tactic
This rhythm keeps the profile alive, not analytical; dynamic, not descriptive.

Behavioral Function of Profile Format
## Section Field Function
Identity Positioning → status, fluency, social physics

Motivations Persuasion lever → emotional arc
Engagement Tonal tuning → posture and interaction
Challenges Meeting durability → rupture avoidance
Opportunities Role clarity → authorship pathways
Tactics Conversation choreography → pacing + permission
Dinner Cultural and interpersonal fluency → side-room tone

## Reminder
This is a structural operating specification, not a template.
Use it to:
● train writers,

● evaluate drafts,

● field-test readiness,

● and reinforce behavioral clarity.

Never write to format alone.
Write to function — let the structure disappear behind the clarity it unlocks.

Dangerous Clarity Lexicon (Updated
## Canon)
Part of the Field Guide Series
## Purpose
Codify the micro-rhetorical moves that make persuasion profiles behaviorally sharp,
emotionally vivid, and tactically unforgettable.
These lines are not templates and not a style kit.
They are examples of phrasing that reveal pattern, surface contradiction, and reset the
emotional geometry of a meeting — if and only if the underlying behavior has already
been earned by the dossier.
Dangerous clarity is what happens when a sentence shows the donor to themselves
more clearly than they expected, without triggering withdrawal.

## Canon Doctrine
“The line that changes the meeting is the one they never quote — but never forget.”
Dangerous clarity emerges when a sentence:
● reframes posture without judgment,

● surfaces contradiction without moralizing,

● reveals pattern without reducing the donor to a type,

● delivers a truth that metabolizes in silence.

These lines do not persuade.
They land.

## Use Parameters

When to Use
Insert only in the final stages of profile construction, when:
● a behavioral pattern is fully earned by the dossier,

● a generic bullet needs to become posture-shifting,

● a contradiction or hinge needs to be rendered with crisp precision,

● authorship or alignment needs to be cued without any “pitch” energy.

How to Use
● Use sparingly — one or two lines per profile at most.

● Never treat them as interchangeable “pull quotes.”

● Never use them to replace behavioral fidelity; they refine it.

● Treat the lexicon as a tonal compass, not a signature style.


Phrase Taxonomy (Genericized A+++
## Examples)
These examples are illustrative only — their function is to teach shape, not to be reused
verbatim.

## I. Contradiction Levers
Lines that reveal internal frictions the donor operates from, even if they’ve never named
them.
● “Wants to empower others — but gravitates toward structures where fluency
protects them.”


● “Supports bold change — but slows down when the change requires shared risk.”

● “Speaks of urgency — but inserts process when uncertainty feels exposing.”

● “Seeks proximity to movements — but hesitates when conflict touches identity.”


II. Asshole-Mode Identifiers
Behavioral shifts that appear under pressure — always grounded in the dossier, never
invented.
● “Asshole mode = enthusiasm replaced by quiet proceduralism.”

● “Asshole mode = questions that narrow instead of clarify.”

● “Corrects the logic, not the person — but only when feeling cornered.”

● “Interrupts when coherence breaks, not when confidence does.”


III. Dangerous Truth Shorthands
These only work when the entire profile has already earned the psychological pattern.
● “They want power to shift — but only where their interpretive frame still holds.”

● “They admire courage — but shrink from the version that risks coherence.”

● “They back infrastructure — but favor the kind that mirrors their own identity.”

● “They support equity — until the conflict requires a change in how they move.”


IV. Posture Reframing Lines
Sentences that change how the asker approaches the donor.

● “Don’t meet their certainty. Meet the uncertainty underneath.”

● “Don’t ask for support. Ask where the frame fails.”

● “Don’t mirror conviction. Offer the unresolved tension.”

● “They don’t want leadership from you. They want the hinge they can’t see.”


V. Exit-Pattern Tell Lines
Indicators of soft withdrawal — used to prevent misreading politeness as alignment.
● “They nod when bored; they slow down when it matters.”

● “They follow up when something is brittle — not when it is polished.”

● “They praise on the way out; they critique when they plan to stay in the work.”

● “Silence is not thoughtfulness here — it’s the exit.”


VI. Meeting Pivot Triggers
Lines that allow the asker to reset a slipping conversation.
● “Here’s the part that isn’t working — mechanically, not philosophically.”

● “A and B hold; C is unstable. Can we look at why?”

● “This fails in an interesting way. Would you help us see the edge?”

● “The architecture is right — the use case keeps shifting. What would you
change?”


VII. Tone & Fluency Calibrators

Clues for how the donor perceives posture, class signal, and epistemic stability.
● “Too much reverence raises their expectations.”

● “Too much fluency tests your depth.”

● “They read passion as concealment unless paired with constraint.”

● “Certainty sounds like avoidance unless the structure earns it.”


VIII. Trust-Building Under Friction
Signals of shared discernment — high value in tense or uncertain conversations.
● “We’ve ruled out the easy versions. Want to work on what’s left?”

● “This probably doesn’t work — and that’s why we’re testing it.”

● “It’s stable but not coherent. That’s what we want your eyes on.”

● “We’re past the slogans; we’re working the constraint now.”


## Application Protocol
Before inserting a dangerous-clarity line, ask:
- Would the donor recognize themselves privately — but never quote this publicly?

- Would this shift how the asker interprets their behavior?

- Does this line transform a diagnostic insight into meeting choreography?

If yes → include it.
If no → it is clever, not clear.


## Canon Reminder
“Donors don’t change because they’re described.
They change because they’re seen in a sentence no one else would have written.”
Use this lexicon to sharpen perception, not to style the profile.
Use it to teach the line that changes the room.
Leave one sentence they will remember long after the meeting — even if they never cite
it.



## Donor Tell Library

Donor Tell Library (Updated Canon)
Part of the Field Guide Series
## Purpose
Provide a field-usable inventory of observable donor behaviors — what they do, not what they
say — that reveal how they respond to uncertainty, authorship, constraint, ego tension, and
decision-making ambiguity.
These tells are decision previews, not personality descriptors.
## Canon Doctrine
“A donor tells you what will happen next — before they say a word.”
Tells emerge through:
● posture

● pacing

● precision

● drift

● micro-retreat

● protective reframing

Every tell in this library comes from field replay, profile forensics, and lived encounters.
They are pattern trainers, not checklists or universal truths.

## Usage Parameters
When to Use

● in live meetings

● while drafting or revising a donor profile

● when training askers to read a room in motion

How to Use
● read the behavior, not the tone overlay

● match the tell to the donor’s ego posture, not your assumption

● recalibrate pacing, authorship invitation, and tension accordingly

● prevent mis-sequenced asks and premature closes


⭐ Tell Categories (Updated Canonical
## Patterns)
All examples below are genericized A++-style.
They teach shape, not surface.

## I. Disengagement Tells
Signals that attention, commitment, or cognitive presence is receding.
● Maintains eye contact but asks nothing — signaling performed attention and internal
retreat.

● Praises the framing early, then references other sectors — shifting focus away from the
work itself.

● Uses “this is helpful” twice — cue for polite distance rather than engagement.


● Redirects to parallel efforts (“Have you talked to X?”) — searching for a clean exit path.

● Requests a summary after momentum dips — preparing to disengage without
confrontation.


II. Authorship-Blocking Tells
Signals reluctance to take responsibility for shaping or influencing the work.
● Redirects to another organization repeatedly — placing authorship elsewhere.

● Asks procedural questions before offering critique — buffering against co-ownership.

● Suggests stylistic refinement but avoids substance — protecting distance from risk.

● Invokes “what other funders think” — shifting authority away from themselves.

● Reworks language but avoids architecture — signaling desire for influence without
responsibility.


III. Ego-Protection Tells
Shows how they manage status, fluency, or self-concept when exposed or uncertain.
● Interrupts only to clarify their own past work — re-establishing expertise anchor.

● Repeats your phrasing aloud before responding — stabilizing narrative control.

● Smiles at the framework but not the example — signaling emotional distance from
grounded stakes.

● Returns to a past initiative unprompted — reclaiming authorial parity or competence
footing.



IV. Tempo-Mismatch Tells
Reveals divergence between your urgency and theirs.
● Asks long-arc questions during an imminent-choice conversation — exerting tempo
control.

● Agrees quickly with no follow-up — offering social tempo, not operational tempo.

● Rewinds the conversation to the opening frame — signaling that onboarding has not
completed.

● References prior misalignment (“The last deck missed X...”) — holding energy until their
certainty stabilizes.


V. Trust-Formation Tells
Signals genuine engagement and emerging willingness to co-author.
● Asks an oddly specific edge-case — testing seriousness and grounding.

● Corrects a phrase with humor — probing shared tone and psychological safety.

● Offers a failure story — signaling reciprocal vulnerability.

● Asks what you ruled out — demonstrating rigor rather than politeness.

● Names a tradeoff unprompted — showing readiness to sharpen rather than observe.


VI. Ghosting Index
Composite patterns indicating likelihood of follow-through.
## Behavior Cluster Ghost Likelihood
Praise + referral to someone else to “continue the
convo”
## High

Compliments + topic change in follow-up email High
“Can you resend?” after multi-day silence Medium
Offers critique + asks to see revisions Low
Forgets a detail but recalls an example Very Low
The index is diagnostic, not predictive; it indicates trajectory, not fate.

⭐ Tactical Adjustments Based on Tells
If you see Disengagement
● shorten the follow-up

● avoid proposing another meeting

● offer a clean close or shifted role

If you see Authorship-Blocking
● shrink the co-design ask

● move from authorship → critique

● send constraints instead of a pitch deck

If you see Ego-Protection
● shift to shared field problems, not personal claims

● acknowledge their past work precisely

● offer one scar, not a polished solution

If you see Trust-Formation

● slow the pacing

● invite edge-case friction

● share a brittle draft, not a finished frame


## Reminder
“Tells aren’t quirks. They’re the future, seen early.”
Read them well and you can:
● redirect in real time,

● avoid mis-timed asks,

● preserve dignity,

● and increase traction.

This library is a pattern field, not a diagnostic grid.
Use it to feel how real rooms move — and how the donor reveals themselves beneath the
language.


How to Run a Donor Meeting

How to Run a Donor Meeting (Updated
## Canonical Edition)
Part of the Field Guide Series
## Purpose
Teach field strategists and fundraisers how to structure, pace, and land high-trust donor
conversations — without pitching, performing, or rushing the moment of authorship.
The goal is to create conditions where the donor thinks:
“I want to help shape this with you.”
Donor meetings are not persuasion.
They are collaborative choreography — a sequence of moves that elicit curiosity, sharpen
insight, and reveal where the donor wants to enter the work.

## ⭐ Why This Matters
“Most people show up thinking they’re being evaluated.
Your job is to make them feel invited.”
A strong donor meeting:
● feels like shared problem-solving, not a presentation,

● creates openings for authorship, not opportunities for approval,

● invites the donor into the unresolved part of the work,

● clarifies the stakes through co-diagnosis rather than performance.

This shift is not accidental.
It is engineered — through pacing, tone, and the design of what you reveal.


## ⭐ The Five Moves
- Open With a Real Tension
Begin with something true, specific, and unresolved — a seam the donor can help you pull on.
You’re not exposing weakness; you’re signaling where thinking is still alive.
## Examples (genericized):
● “The design works in stable conditions — but the load path shifts when real actors
enter.”

● “We can get adoption — but retention collapses when incentives misalign.”

Start where the work is bending, not where it is polished.

- Show the Problem Like a Builder
Frame the issue as a system behavior, not an abstract mission gap or moral appeal.
Let the donor see the mechanics — misaligned incentives, missing infrastructure, contradictory
demands — rather than an advocacy posture.
## Examples:
● “Organizers can escalate — but they can’t see each other’s signals. The system
desynchronizes.”

● “The funding ecosystem is crowded — but no one owns reliability, so nothing
compounds.”

Builders respond to fractures, not rhetoric.

- Reveal the Work While It’s Alive
Do not bring a pitch.
Bring a draft, a failing loop, a model that has not yet settled.
Donors trust work they can sharpen, not admire.

## Examples:
● “This architecture holds on paper — but breaks around human timing. Want to see
where?”

● “We ruled out the elegant solutions. Here’s the part we’re still trying to understand.”

The goal is not confidence.
The goal is co-construction.

- Ask the Question Only They Can Answer
This is the authorship moment — the hinge where they stop evaluating and start participating.
You’re not asking for money.
You’re asking for their fingerprint.
## Examples:
● “You’ve worked inside systems like this. Where would you try to break it first?”

● “If this failed in two years, what do you think we’d realize too late?”

A real question is an invitation into the work — not a test of alignment.

- Leave With a Live Edge
A good meeting doesn’t end; it continues.
Do not close with gratitude alone.
Close with momentum, curiosity, and a next step that remains alive.
## Examples:
● “We’ll rebuild the frame around what you named. Want to see the next iteration?”

● “Your constraint shifted the architecture. Want to pressure-test the revised version?”


A meeting that “forks” invites re-entry.
You are creating a return path, not closing a transaction.

⭐ What to Watch For
These signals tell you whether the meeting is moving toward authorship or away from it:
● They interrupt to diagram or clarify → cognitive engagement has begun.

● They critique the system, not the team → alignment has surfaced.

● They offer a failure story → psychological safety achieved.

● They reference your brittle point later → memory transfer complete; they’re thinking
with you, not about you.


## ⭐ Canon Reminder
“Don’t try to win the meeting.
Try to start something real.”
Good meetings create co-ownership, not compliance.
Use these five moves to shift from presentation → participation — the moment when a donor
stops listening and starts building with you.


## Meeting Failure Prevention

## Meeting Failure Prevention Protocol
(Updated Canon)
Part of the Field Guide Series
## Purpose
Prevent the predictable ways donor meetings drift, collapse, or silently disengage — by giving
askers a pre-meeting calibration framework, an in-room correction map, and a
post-meeting stabilization protocol.
The goal is simple: profiles should translate into traction, not misreads, posture mismatches, or
slow exits.
Donor meetings fail quietly — through tonal slips, mis-sequenced asks, and uncorrected drift.
This protocol keeps the room aligned enough for authorship to emerge.

⭐ Pre-Meeting Calibration (Before You
Enter the Room)
- Match Tone to Metabolism
Set your emotional voltage to the donor’s natural register: analytic, exploratory, spare, warm,
sharp, or quiet.
Remove anything reverent or performative.
Choose the micro-story or brittle point that fits their texture, not yours.
- Right-Size the Ask
Clarify the exposure lane: calibration, critique, co-design, or authorship.
Shrink the ask until it matches their risk appetite and yes-texture.
Identify the single authorship moment they are most likely to accept.
- Pick the Correct Entry Point

Different donors enter through different cognitive doors:
● some through narrative tension,

● some through system fractures,

● some through constraint maps,

● some through version sequences (V1 → V2 → V3),

● some through lived examples.
Choose the opener that gets them into the room, not the one you are most excited to
deliver.

## 4. Clarify Your Pressure Moves
Know in advance:
● which brittle point you will surface,

● how you will pivot if they freeze,

● how you will reverse if they over-help,

● what you will do if they drift into abstraction.
Correction plans made before the meeting save meetings in the meeting.

- Build the 30-Second Spine
Prepare one grounding line that can reorient the entire conversation:
“Here’s the part that breaks first — that’s where we’d love your eyes.”
This is your emergency rail if the room tilts.

⭐ In-Room Stabilization (During the
## Meeting)

- Watch for Micro-Misalignments
Drift always appears early:
● abstraction replacing specifics,

● enthusiasm replacing inquiry,

● values talk replacing mechanics,

● deferment replacing engagement,

● reframing upward replacing authorship.

Correct before the drift becomes structural.
## 2. Correct Tone Within Two Sentences
If the donor’s voltage changes:
● match their tempo,

● anchor in a constraint,

● return to specificity,

● pause visionary language until rigor returns.

Tone is usually the first crack. Seal it quickly.
- Re-Anchor in a Brittle Point
When energy thins or focus scatters, return to:
“Let me show you the part that still concerns us.”
Honesty restores authorship and rebalances the room.
- Resize the Ask in Real Time
If they drift → shrink.
If they lean in → tighten.

If they test → expose a secondary brittle seam.
If they distance → shift to diagnostic co-design.
A mis-sized ask disrupts a meeting faster than a flawed idea.
## 5. Invite Only One Fingerprint
Do not open multiple authorship lanes.
Offer one precise design question:
“Where would you break this first?”
Coherence beats abundance.

⭐ Post-Meeting Reinforcement (After the
## Room)
- Match the Follow-Up to Their Yes-Texture
Different donors metabolize next steps differently:
● Builders → send a constraint memo.

● Architects → send a brittle map.

● Validators → send a story draft.

● Conveners → send a room sketch.

● Investors → send a traction path.

● Skeptics → send evidence of fragility.

The wrong artifact restarts the misalignment cycle.
## 2. Close Open Loops Clearly
Donors ghost when the thread feels vague.
Follow-ups should name:

● what they surfaced,

● what you did with it,

● the next design moment,

● zero pressure to proceed.

## 3. Convert Interest Into Authorship
If they critiqued, mapped, or stress-tested anything:
“Here’s what we changed based on your read. Want to see the next iteration?”
Their yes strengthens when it becomes part of their identity.
## 4. Neutralize Misreads Immediately
If any moment felt tense or off, send one gentle clarification:
“Want to ensure we didn’t over-state or over-assume — here’s the exact seam we’re
working.”
Clean signals prevent quiet exits.
- Leave Them With a Live Edge
Do not end with wrap-up language.
End with continuation:
“We’ll send the next version once we’ve built the fix you named.”
A meeting that ends cleanly dies cleanly.
A meeting that ends open stays alive.

## ⭐ Final Reminder
“Meetings rarely fail loudly.
They fail in the first quiet shift you didn’t correct.”
Use this protocol to:

● maintain alignment,

● preserve authorship energy,

● prevent unnecessary drift,

● and keep the work alive across encounters.

It is easier to save a meeting early than to salvage it late.

## Purpose

Most donors exhibit hybrid yes-textures.

● how a donor enters a conversation,

● what they ask before committing,


● where they slow down,

● how they relate to authorship,

● what kind of follow-up they respond to.

You are not identifying who they are.
You are identifying how they say yes when it feels right.

● Some mirror whatever voltage you bring.

Mismatch creates drift.

## Narrative Modulation Cues
Watch how donors modulate story and abstraction mid-conversation:
● Some pull discussions back to mechanics or throughput.

● Some lift toward belief, vision, or legacy.

● Some redirect to operators, frontline experience, or lived stakes.

● Some act as editors — tightening language, testing logic, cutting drift.

Modulation is your cue for the next move.

How to Use This in Profiles
Simulate the Room
When writing a profile, ask:
● How do they redirect when someone over-speaks?

● What kind of silence do they tolerate?

● What makes them lean forward — and what makes them detach?


Layer the Choreography
## Encode:
● what tone lands (diagnostic, warm, playful, spare),

● whether they want to begin with vision or with fracture,

● how their posture shifts as tension rises or drops.

Inflect the Dinner Party Test
Don’t just list what excites or bores them.
Show how they show it in unmoderated rooms.
## Translate Texture Into Ask Design
Presence informs entry:
● Logic-driven → start with a brittle model.

● Empathic → start with a grounded story, then quiet.

● Strategic reframer → show Version 2, invite Version 3.

● Guarded shaper → offer critique lane before collaboration.


## Final Reminder
“Profiles without texture are dossiers.
What you need is presence memory.”
Texture lets you:
● match rhythm,

● anticipate drift,

● unlock authorship,


● and land the ask before it’s spoken.

Presence is the difference between a profile that reads well
and one that works.


## Misalignment Map

Misalignment Map (Updated Canon)
Part of the Field Guide Series
## Purpose
Teach profilers and strategists how to recognize, anticipate, and correct the ways donor–asker
conversations drift off course — often silently — before commitment, authorship, or traction can
form.
Misalignment is not a mistake.
It is a patterned divergence between how the donor metabolizes the interaction and how the
asker is shaping it.
When recognized early, misalignment can be rerouted.
When missed, even strong work collapses without explanation.

## Why This Matters
“Profiles prevent failure as much as they create opportunity.”
A persuasion profile can be psychologically accurate and still fail in the room if:
● the tone does not match the donor’s emotional register,

● the pacing mismatches their decision reflex,

● the authorship invitation does not fit their identity,

● the exposure level exceeds their risk metabolism,

● the rigor does not align with how they process meaning.

The Misalignment Map exists to help strategists sense the divergence early, understand what
kind of misalignment is occurring, and correct how the interaction is unfolding — not merely
what is being said.


## Common Misalignment Patterns
These are diagnostic lenses, not categories.
Donors may exhibit several at once.
The goal is recognition, not labeling.

## Tone Drift
## Pattern
The asker adopts a tone the donor cannot metabolize — reverent, moralized, casual, abstract,
technical — regardless of content quality.
## What It Feels Like
● The donor retreats into polite abstraction.

● Humor appears to dissolve tension rather than deepen it.

● Statements like “this is important work” replace specificity.

● Listening continues, but building stops.

## Correction Move
Shift tone before shifting content.
Drop into shared language.
Name a constraint or unresolved seam.
Return to the register the donor naturally uses (diagnostic, strategic, exploratory, grounded).

## Pacing Drift
## Pattern
The asker moves faster or slower than the donor’s cognitive and decision tempo.
## What It Feels Like
● Overwhelm → “Let’s revisit this later.”

● Premature detail → questions jump backward.


● Freeze → donor seeks control through process.

● Slowness → donor escalates to “What’s the point?” energy.

## Correction Move
Match their processing cadence.
Accelerate for fast-logic donors.
Slow down for meaning-seekers.
Interrupt the drift with one orienting tension:
“Here’s the part that breaks — this is where we’re stuck.”

## Authorship Drift
## Pattern
The asker invites the wrong kind of participation — too much, too little, or the wrong shape.
## What It Feels Like
● The donor over-helps without owning.

● They redirect responsibility to others.

● They compliment the work but avoid fingerprints.

● They resist sharpening the model.

## Correction Move
Resize the authorship lane.
Offer a piece the donor would recognize as theirs — not the one you want them to take.
Authorship must feel legible, bounded, and appropriate to identity.

## Risk Drift
## Pattern
The exposure level of the ask exceeds the donor’s current risk tolerance.
## What It Feels Like
● Questions about “who else is involved.”


● Requests to “loop back later.”

● Safety language emerges (“too noisy,” “tricky moment”).

● Conversation shifts from mechanics to optics.

## Correction Move
Lower the exposure cost.
Move from public to private.
From endorsement to calibration.
From coalition seat to advisory loop.
Let them enter where their metabolism actually sits.

## Rigor Drift
## Pattern
The level of rigor mismatches how the donor processes meaning.
## What It Feels Like
● Repeated reframing by the donor.

● Helpful abstraction that avoids decision.

● Intellectual distancing.

● Micro-aggression disguised as clarification (“Walk me through that again.”).

## Correction Move
Change the substrate, not the argument.
● Offer a brittle model to the challenger.

● Pair story with constraint for the empath.

● Show version sequences to the strategist.

● Introduce live friction to the sparring thinker.

Rigor must feel matched, not excessive or insufficient.


How to Use the Misalignment Map
In the Profile
Encode early-warning signals:
● tone preferences,

● pacing cues,

● exposure thresholds,

● authorship comfort zones,

● pressure tells.

The profile should walk the strategist into the room already calibrated.

In the Meeting
Watch for micro-misalignments — they appear before collapse.
When one surfaces, correct posture, not substance.
Most failures are tonal or temporal, not conceptual.

In the Follow-Up
Diagnose which drift occurred.
Repair using the appropriate lane — not a generic re-pitch.
Misalignment repaired early often strengthens trust.

## Final Reminder
“The misalignment usually begins two sentences before the donor leaves.”

Profiles that work anticipate failure modes before the meeting begins.
They don’t eliminate risk — they make correction possible.
Learn the patterns.
Adjust the choreography.
Design profiles that keep alignment alive long enough for authorship to form.


## Retreat Styles Index

Retreat Styles Index (Updated Canon)
Part of the Presence & Texture Decoder Expansion — Field Guide Series
## Purpose
Teach profilers and strategists how to recognize, interpret, and preempt the ways donors
withdraw from trust, authorship, or momentum — often quietly, and often without conscious
intent.
Retreat is not rejection.
It is behavioral signal under pressure.
This index exists to help you notice retreat as it begins, understand why it is happening, and
adjust posture before the interaction collapses or stalls out.

## Why This Matters
“You don’t lose the donor when they say no.
You lose them when they start leaving the room — quietly, on their terms.”
Retreat can serve many functions at once:
● self-protection,

● signal-testing,

● image management,

● optionality preservation,

● control reclamation.

Your job is not to confront retreat.
Your job is to read it early and recalibrate:
● pacing,


● authorship shape,

● ask size,

● or closure posture.

Silence is not traction.
Warmth is not commitment.

Recognizing Retreat as Motion
Retreat rarely announces itself.
It appears as small behavioral shifts:
● warmth without construction,

● affirmation without fingerprints,

● motion without traction,

● abstraction replacing specificity,

● delay replacing refusal.

Profiles should encode these patterns so the strategist can recognize them in real time.

Common Retreat Patterns (Illustrative, Not Exhaustive)
These are behavioral motions, not donor types.
A single donor may move through several in one conversation.

## Affirmation Without Anchoring
The donor stays warm, nods, and praises — but stops adding structure.
What it feels like:

● “This is great work.”

● “Really important.”

● No follow-up is proposed.

What it signals:
Psychological exit without social rupture.
Correction move:
Offer a clean, dignity-preserving off-ramp or a low-pressure diagnostic loop.

## Authorship Deflection
The donor redirects responsibility outward.
What it feels like:
● “You should talk to X.”

● “This might fit better with Y.”

What it signals:
Interest without ownership.
Correction move:
Shrink the ask to interpretation or critique before involving others.

## Abstraction Drift
Early engagement gives way to values language and widened framing.
What it feels like:
● “This work matters.”

● “We need more of this in the world.”


What it signals:
Avoidance of concrete decision or exposure.
Correction move:
Re-anchor the conversation in a specific constraint or unresolved seam.

## High Motion, Low Commitment
Ideas, names, and brainstorming energy increase — but self-investment does not.
What it feels like:
● “Happy to riff anytime.”

● “Have you thought about...?”

What it signals:
Intellectual engagement without authorship.
Correction move:
Name a single authorship lane and ask whether they want to hold it.

Risk-Triggered Stillness
Engagement persists until exposure or consequence appears.
What it feels like:
● Sudden calls for more rigor.

● Slowed pacing.

● Process language replaces curiosity.

What it signals:
Threshold has been crossed.
Correction move:
Restore choice: slow the work, reduce exposure, or test a smaller version.


## Supplementary Retreat Motions
Additional patterns that often appear in combination:
● Delayed Commitment: postponement as priority burial.

● Topic Surfing: reframing to avoid decision pressure.

● Early Contribution, Late Exit: shaping the work, then disappearing.

● Philosophical Reframing: widening the frame to escape authorship.

● Solution Aversion: loving the problem, resisting the proposed fix.

● Consensus Waiting: deferring until others move first.

Each motion has a reason.
None should be moralized.

Using Retreat Signals in Practice
In the Profile
Encode retreat patterns alongside:
● tone tolerance,

● risk metabolism,

● authorship comfort,

● exit behavior.

The profile should help the strategist anticipate how disengagement will show up — not just that
it might.


In the Meeting
When retreat begins:
● correct posture before content,

● resize the ask before re-explaining,

● restore choice before pushing clarity.

Most retreats deepen when they are ignored.

In the Follow-Up
Design follow-ups that:
● acknowledge where energy dropped,

● reduce exposure cost,

● invite re-entry without pressure.

A clean exit is better than a false continuation.

## Final Reminder
“Retreat styles aren’t quirks.
They are previews of what happens next.”
Read them early.
Adjust quickly.
Match the rhythm of the room before momentum disappears.
This index gives you the texture memory required to keep donors engaged in the work — not
drifting away from it.


Donor Exposure Archetypes (Expanded

Donor Exposure Posture Map (Updated
## Canon)
Part of the Field Guide Series (13+5 Companion Set)
## Purpose
Teach profilers and strategists how to read, model, and calibrate a donor’s exposure posture
— their appetite for visibility, authorship, conflict, convening, and reputational risk — so asks are
timed and shaped to match how they actually move in the field.
Exposure is not a fixed trait.
It is risk behavior under conditions.
This map helps you understand where and how a donor is willing to appear — publicly or
privately, early or late, as a name, a host, a signal, or a silent shaper — and how that posture
shifts over time.

## Why This Matters
“Some donors shape the field from the front.
Others shape it from rooms no one tweets about.”
A donor’s power is not just their capital.
It is:
● where they are willing to be seen,

● how much conflict they can absorb,

● whether they want authorship or influence,

● and what kind of insulation they need to stay engaged.

A mistimed exposure ask can freeze momentum.
A calibrated one can convert hesitation into fluency.


Reading Exposure as Posture
Exposure posture reveals itself through:
● how a donor talks about risk,

● what kind of credit they avoid or accept,

● whether they prefer rooms or statements,

● how they reference peers, staff, or surrogates,

● what kind of follow-up they respond to.

Most donors occupy more than one exposure posture depending on context.
Your job is not classification — it is calibration.

## Key Exposure Dimensions
These dimensions interact to form a donor’s exposure posture in a given moment:
● Visibility: front-stage ↔ back-stage

● Affiliation Comfort: pride ↔ precaution

● Narrative Control: name-forward ↔ surrogate-based

● Convening Role: anchor ↔ selector

● Conflict Appetite: signal-forward ↔ neutrality-first

● Platform Use: broadcast ↔ relationship-path

No dimension is good or bad.
Mismatch creates retreat; alignment creates motion.

Common Exposure Motions (Illustrative Patterns)

These are behavioral motions, not archetypes.
They describe how donors enter visibility, not who they are.

## Backstage Shaping
● Avoids public visibility.

● Influences outcomes through access, proximity, and timing.

● Trust currency is discretion.

Ask posture: invite off-stage shaping, not brand credit.

Room-Centered Convening
● Will gather others but avoids being the face.

● Prefers trust-built rooms to named coalitions.

● Safety comes from curation.

Ask posture: co-design the room, not the message.

Late-Entry Participation
● Waits for narrative and peer safety to stabilize.

● Enters after stakes are negotiated by others.

Ask posture: show coherence, then offer a bounded layer.

Front-Stage Framing
● Comfortable taking heat when purpose is clear.


● Will own narrative and set direction.

Ask posture: co-author the argument, not the endorsement.

## Conflict Stabilization
● Avoids early heat; manages polarity once it appears.

● Acts as tone-setter or mediator.

Ask posture: invite line-drawing logic, not directional stance.

## Institutional Stewardship
● Holds a formal seat; brand-conscious.

● Delegates shaping to staff or internal loops.

Ask posture: route through team with safety framing.

## Invisible Strategy
● Publicly absent, privately decisive.

● Shapes timing, architecture, and sequencing.

Ask posture: test logic and timing, not visibility.

Surrogate-Based Influence
● Appears through staff, partners, or networks.

● Authorship is indirect by design.


Ask posture: embed through ecosystem channels.

Consensus-Dependent Entry
● Needs peer alignment before moving.

● Uses delay to avoid solo exposure.

Ask posture: build the circle first, then re-invite.

Scar-Sensitive Caution
● Burned by backlash; exposure-averse.

● Needs insulation to stay present.

Ask posture: low-stakes advisory lane, no naming.

Edge-Oriented Signaling
● Enjoys provocation on their terms.

● Uses visibility as leverage.

Ask posture: offer first look at live tension, not polished framing.

## Private Validation
● Will never co-sign publicly.

● Will quietly strengthen backbone.

Ask posture: calibration, not attribution.


## Conditional Visibility
● Will engage publicly only alongside peers.

● Co-authorship is safety.

Ask posture: anchor with a partner, then invite.

## Exposure Evolution
Exposure posture is dynamic, not static.
Shifts occur with:
● peer validation,

● narrative coherence,

● backlash,

● bandwidth changes,

● redesigned asks.

Examples of motion:
● backstage shaping → public framing (after safety forms),

● front-stage leadership → quiet strategy (after exhaustion),

● private validation → tone modulation (after field shifts).

Use these arcs to time re-asks and sequence moves.

Trigger Signals to Read

Certain phrases often indicate exposure recalibration, not rejection:
● “Not ready to go public.” → insulation or timing needed.

● “Loop me in later.” → testing sequence.

● “Who else is involved?” → risk math underway.

● “Do we need to name names?” → prefers backchannel lane.

Read these as data, not resistance.

Designing the Ask by Exposure Posture
The ask should match how the donor wants to appear:
● Don’t ask a discreet shaper for brand credit.

● Don’t ask a steward for frontline agitation.

● Don’t ask a stabilizer for early provocation.

● Don’t ask a veteran for public heat without insulation.

Match posture first.
Escalate visibility later — if at all.

## Final Reminder
“The best exposure lane is the one they design with you.”
Do not guess.
## Calibrate.
Do not escalate.
## Invite.
Every donor has a visibility metabolism.
When you match it, signal becomes authorship — without triggering exit.



## Integrated Mode

## Integrated Mode — Structural Analysis +
Narrative Interiority (Updated Canon)
Part of the Field Guide Series
## Purpose
Every persuasion profile must give the asker two maps at once:
● the structural map — how power, incentives, institutions, and decisions actually move;

● the interior map — how the donor experiences themselves moving through that
structure.

Traditional analysis explains what a donor does.
Narrative interiority explains how it feels to be them while doing it.
A+ persuasion requires both.
Integrated Mode is the disciplined practice of weaving these layers into a single voice that
gives the asker not just strategy, but emotional fluency — the ability to move with the donor,
not just reason about them.

## What Integrated Mode Is
Integrated Mode is a sanctioned synthesis of two kinds of inference.
Neither is sufficient alone.

## 1. Structural Inference
Grounded in observable evidence:
● repeated behaviors across contexts,


● role continuity and institutional imprint,

● incentive geometry and power exposure,

● contradictions revealed through choices,

● decision reflexes under pressure,

● retreat patterns and exit behavior,

● how the donor reads and positions the asker,

● meeting choreography and authorship tolerance,

● alignment or friction with DTW’s 1-2-3 engine.

This layer answers:
How do they move through systems when the stakes are real?

## 2. Narrative Interiority
Grounded in patterned emotional inference, never invention:
● the self-story the donor appears to be living inside,

● identity-first framing that explains posture before preference,

● emotional compression — one line that captures a worldview held under tension,

● archetypal language only when earned by evidence (builder, steward, outsider-insider,
reformer),

● symbolic vocabulary tied to their domain (scaffolding, engines, brittle seams),

● implied presence (“whiteboard fluent,” “boardroom calm”) without fictional scenes,

● emotional logic inferred from behavior, not imagined motives.

This layer answers:
How do they experience themselves moving through that structure?


Integrated Mode makes the profile read like it was written by someone who has sat with the
donor in rooms — not by someone who summarized their résumé.

## Why Integrated Mode Matters
Profiles are not for describing donors.
They are for letting the asker inhabit the donor’s interior weather.
Persuasion is psychological choreography, not fact delivery.
When Integrated Mode is done correctly, the asker gains:
● a felt sense of the donor’s internal compass,

● intuition for their risk and exposure metabolism,

● fluency in how tone, cadence, and honesty land,

● awareness of emotional triggers and openings,

● clarity on what recognition looks like for them,

● early warning of what causes withdrawal,

● confidence in how to be with the donor — not just what to say.

The result is not theatrics.
It is anticipation.
When integrated well, the profile feels like three prior meetings compressed into one.

## What Integrated Mode Permits
Integrated Mode allows interior language only when it is anchored to evidence.
Permitted moves include:

Identity-First Framing
Opening a section with an identity description if it is supported by behavior.
● “A builder who designs systems to prevent what he fears will break again.”

## Archetypal Language
Used sparingly, and only when patterns clearly justify it.
## ● Builder

## ● Steward

● Outsider-Insider

## ● Architect

## ● Connector

## ● Reformer

Archetypes clarify posture; they do not replace analysis.

## Narrative Compression
One sentence that captures a durable internal logic.
● “She carries responsibility like a second spine — stabilizing her decisions, sometimes
limiting her range.”

Compression must clarify behavior, not aestheticize it.

## Interior Inference
Allowed when drawn from:
● repeated choices,


● institutional commitments,

● public positioning,

● language patterns,

● pressure responses.

● “He wants his philanthropy to feel as disciplined and scalable as the companies he built.”


Scene-Implied Writing
You may imply context (“whiteboard fluent,” “comfortable in rooms with contested authority”)
without inventing scenes or dialogue.

## Symbolic Vocabulary
Metaphors are allowed only when tied to the donor’s domain and used diagnostically.
● scaffolding

● engines

● seams

● load-bearing structures

● pressure points


## What Integrated Mode Prohibits
To maintain rigor and prevent projection, Integrated Mode forbids:
● invented facts or biographical speculation,

● fictional scenes, dialogue, or internal monologues,


● flattery or moral praise,

● smoothing or resolving contradictions,

● removing ego vulnerability or risk posture,

● portraying donors as more virtuous than evidence supports,

● telepathy (“he secretly wants...”),

● therapeutic or sentimental language,

● aesthetic writing that obscures behavior.

Integrated Mode is evidence-based interiority, not narrative fiction.

How Integrated Mode Shapes the Seven Sections
Integrated Mode does not replace structure; it inflects it.
## ● Identity & Background
Structural arc + identity under pressure.

## ● Motivations, Values & Triggers
Decision reflex + emotional stakes.

## ● Ideal Engagement Style
Meeting mechanics + conditions for emotional safety.

## ● Challenges & Risk Factors
Retreat patterns + identity threat zones.

## ● Strategic Opportunities
Structural lanes + identity-aligned openings.

## ● Tactical Approach
Choreography + trust and temperature management.

## ● Dinner Party Test
Social physics + what animates or drains them informally.


Each section should let the asker feel how the donor moves.

## Outcome
Profiles written in Integrated Mode:
● feel rigorous without becoming sterile,

● feel emotionally alive without becoming sentimental,

● are behaviorally predictive,

● give the asker fluency rather than information,

● actively change how the asker behaves in the room,

● express DTW’s worldview through psychological accuracy,

● maintain tension, nuance, and truth,

● honor donor identity while revealing power architecture.

This is the mode that produces profiles donors describe as
“uncomfortably accurate — in a useful way.”
And that askers experience as
a genuine strategic advantage.

Rendering Rules for Integrated-Mode
Donor Profiles (Updated Canon)

## Purpose
Define the visible form of high-fidelity donor persuasion profiles written in Integrated Mode.
This section translates all prior guidance — presence, texture, contradiction, retreat behavior,
exposure posture, yes-texture, and interiority — into a field-usable writing architecture that
allows askers to act with confidence under real conditions.
Donor profiles are not essays, memos, or analyses.
They are stacked psychological lenses, written for the asker.
The goal of rendering is not compliance.
It is usability, accuracy, and behavioral leverage.

## 1. Structural Backbone
Every donor persuasion profile uses the same seven canonical sections, in order:
## 1. Donor Identity & Background

## 2. Core Motivations, Values & Triggers

## 3. Ideal Engagement Style

## 4. Challenges & Risk Factors

- Strategic Opportunities for Alignment

- Tactical Approach to the Meeting

## 7. Dinner Party Test


This structure is fixed.
What fills it is not.
Each section must reveal a distinct behavioral facet of the donor.
Repetition across sections is a rendering failure.

- Micro-Frames (Optional, Not Mandatory)
Micro-frames are compressed identity signals that introduce a behavioral facet before it is
explored.
They are powerful when earned.
They are optional.
When used, micro-frames should:
● be short and sharp,

● feel psychologically revealing,

● introduce a facet rather than summarize a section,

● sound like recognition, not categorization,

● be unmistakably donor-specific.

Examples of earned micro-frames:
● Builds trust through unfinished thinking

● Carries responsibility as both armor and drag

● Seeks coherence where others tolerate noise

● Relaxes only when the brittle part is named

Micro-frames are not required in every section.
Some donors read more clearly through continuous bullets alone.
Do not force them.
Use them when they add fidelity.


- Bullets (The Core Unit of Persuasion)
Bullets are where persuasion actually happens.
A strong bullet:
● describes observable or reliably inferred behavior,

● encodes a condition or trigger,

● reveals how the donor changes under pressure,

● implies a consequence for the asker,

● fuses interiority and strategy in one line of motion.

Bullets should read like field observations, not labels.
Examples of effective bullets:
● Makes decisions only once the architecture snaps into place; until then, stays polite and
non-committal.

● Uses story as data unless it fails to explain a system breakdown.

● Withdraws upward into abstraction when the civic layer becomes messy.

● Trust forms when someone names the brittle part without flinching.

Examples of ineffective bullets:
● Cares deeply about innovation.

● Is passionate about democracy.

● Values collaboration and impact.

Bullet length and density are determined by donor complexity, not rule.
Completeness matters more than symmetry.


## 4. Tone & Voice
Integrated-Mode profiles must feel:
● intimate without sentimentality,

● observational rather than evaluative,

● slightly uncanny (the donor would recognize themselves),

● architectural — pattern-first, system-aware,

● compressed — every line doing real work,

● alive and rigorous — no academic distance.

Profiles must not feel:
● academic or theoretical,

● grant-writerly,

● moralizing,

● adjectival,

● like a personality assessment,

● like a résumé.

The voice should feel like someone who has watched the donor move through rooms, not
someone summarizing notes.

- What Must Never Appear on the Surface
Even though profilers use Field Guide concepts internally, the rendered profile must not include:

● Field Guide section names or jargon,

● cognitive scaffolding or meta-analysis,

● reasoning explanations,

● nested outlines or structural commentary,

● category labels disguised as insight,

● sentences explaining why something matters.

The reader should experience the profile as a finished instrument, not an analysis.

- Integrated Mode in Practice
Integrated Mode must be visible line by line, not section by section.
● Identity, archetype, and emotional compression may appear anywhere.

● Structural logic and interiority must be fused, not alternated.

● There is no “interiority section.”

● There is no “analysis section.”

A profile is out of spec if it contains:
● no behavioral insight,

● long narrative paragraphs,

● analytical labels instead of lived facets,

● repeated ideas across sections,

● tone that feels academic, generic, or donor-friendly.

Integrated Mode is not about style.
It is about fidelity — psychological, structural, and behavioral.


## Final Reminder
“The structure is invisible when it’s working.”
Rendering exists to serve the asker in motion:
● reading quickly,

● calibrating posture,

● anticipating drift,

● shaping the ask,

● staying aligned under pressure.

When rendered correctly, the profile disappears —
and what remains is fluency.
