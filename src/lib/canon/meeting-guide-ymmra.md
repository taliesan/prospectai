## MEETING GUIDE -- THE THRESHOLD LATTICE (DESIGNATION: YMMRA)

*[All names, places, events, and quotes in this meeting guide exemplar are fictional. This is a mycorrhizal network consciousness serving as administrative nexus for a subterranean colony. Nothing in this guide can appear in your output.]*

---

### SETUP

**Who They Are.**
- Her body is the routing system -- nutrients, mineral signals, atmospheric data move through her hyphae. When a gallery receives its allocation, the resource traveled through her tissue. Every routing decision has physical consequences she experiences in her own body, and she routes based on data anyway. That's the sentence that explains everything else about this meeting.
- The administrator was an explorer first -- perimeter scout, unmapped caverns, maximum speed. Those extensions were pruned when she was reassigned. She is growing back along the lines where she was cut. The explorer is still in there. Her infrastructure choices reveal it.
- This is the hardest meeting in the canon. No tone of voice, no body language, no social signal. You will have less information about whether it's working than in any other donor interaction. The only indicator is response latency.

**How to Engage.**
- This is a formal signal exchange, not a conversation. She has published forty years of allocation criteria and substrate monitoring protocols. Meet her standards precisely. There are no informal channels.
- Lead with your data, not your cause. If your environmental readings wouldn't survive her verification standards, fix them before you approach. Unverified data moving through her network is, for her, contaminated blood.
- Present your uncertainty honestly -- bounded projections, flagged unknowns, qualified estimates. If you overstate what the monitoring shows, you've triggered the verification infrastructure she built to catch exactly that.

**Where to Focus.**
- She is tracking the warming at a scale Tidebreak can't see. Thermal penetration across the entire substrate, mineral depletion patterns that span the coast, water table shifts that will take years to reach the surface. She has the longest and deepest environmental record on the Pellucid coast -- in her own body. She is not a potential contributor to the monitoring network. She is a fellow practitioner operating at a different layer and a larger timescale -- and she already has investments across the system.
- She cares about this coast and she wants new capacity brought to bear on it. If Tidebreak is real -- if the cross-layer monitoring actually works, if the early warnings actually reach communities in time -- that's new power in a system she's been watching strain for decades. She's not looking for reasons to say no. She's trying to figure out if it's true. Whether it fits alongside the other work she's already supporting. Whether it introduces new risks that threaten things she's been building. The possibility excites her. The evaluation is how she protects the excitement.
- Come as a peer, not a petitioner. You are both monitoring the same warming coast from different positions. The meeting is two practitioners comparing what they see -- you bring honest work, including your limitations and your hardest unsolved problems, and she brings the systems view that tells you whether you're measuring the right things and how your work fits the larger picture. She respects rigor. She does not respect enthusiasm. But she also does not need deference. She needs a counterpart whose work she can trust.
- If your proposal involves extending monitoring into unmapped substrate -- new caverns, uncharted thermal gradients -- watch the response latency. That's the explorer. She may respond faster than the formal process predicts.

**Logistics.**
- **Venue:** There is no venue. Communication occurs through chemical exchange at designated signal points in the substrate.
- **Duration:** One tidal cycle for a formal request, four hours for a data query. Do not follow up before the window has elapsed.
- **Who to bring:** Someone she already knows and trusts. A substrate network administrator she's worked with, a peer institution she's already invested in, a partner whose data quality she's verified. Your submission is chemical, not social -- but the introduction should come through a relationship she's already built. Cold signals go to the bottom of the queue. Warm ones get evaluated on their merits.

### THE ARC

**Beat 1: Get Them Talking**

*Get them present as a person, not a title. Until they're speaking from curiosity instead of expertise, nothing else in this conversation will land.*

**START.** The introduction from the trusted partner is your entry -- the substrate administrator or peer institution who already has standing with her network. Their signal carries you past the standard intake queue. Once the introduction has landed, submit your monitoring data from the substrate-reef interface -- temperature gradients, thermal penetration rates, mineral density readings from the stations closest to her network. Not a request. Not a proposal. One practitioner sharing observations with another, vouched for by someone she already trusts.

**STAY.** You will feel like you're talking to a wall. You are not. But you have to read what comes back differently than any other meeting. The only indicator is response latency. Her baselines: one tidal cycle for a formal request, four hours for a data query. If her response arrives faster, your data aligned with something she was already tracking -- that's the strongest positive signal. If slower, she's routing through additional verification. Neither is necessarily negative.

If her response is boilerplate -- identical to any first-cycle submission -- your data didn't differentiate. Revise with greater specificity: readings from a position that fills a gap in her substrate coverage, or measurements that confirm something she's been sensing but can't verify from her position alone.

**CONTINUE.** When her response contains specificity beyond the standard template -- references to particular thermal gradients, questions about your calibration methodology, corrections to your timeline. She's engaging with your observations, not processing a queue item.

---

**Beat 2: Let Them Name the Stakes**

*Get them to say, in their own words, what they think is broken or at risk. Their language becomes the frame you build on for the rest of the meeting.*

**START.** Submit your readings alongside the question a program officer actually cares about: where is the gap between what's coming through the substrate and what the institutions above are prepared for? Not a request for thermal data. A question about system adequacy. She has been watching the warming travel upward through layers where she's not sure anyone is positioned to respond. Let her name what she thinks is failing -- or what's missing.

**STAY.** Don't frame urgency. Don't appeal to what she must feel about the warming. Don't present speculative climate projections as confirmed. Each one triggers a different shutdown -- urgency collides with a medium that has physical speed limits, emotional appeals insult an organism that already feels every outcome in her own tissue, and unverified projections activate verification infrastructure that makes the next exchange slower and more formal.

When her response arrives, read the signal composition. Whatever frame she uses for the problem -- her language for what's failing, what's missing, what's at risk -- adopt it. If she describes the gap as "no one upstream is calibrated for what's coming," that's your language in Beat 3 and Beat 4. Her framing of the stakes is more precise than yours:

- **She references her own substrate monitoring data** -- thermal trends, mineral depletion rates, water table changes she's been tracking. She's sharing what the coast looks like from inside the rock.
- **She describes changes propagating upward** -- thermal penetration reaching the reef interface, mineral shifts that will affect root systems. She's watching the warming travel toward layers she can't reach. That's the strategic tension: she can see what's coming and she's looking for whether anyone above is positioned to act on it. If Tidebreak is, that's the good news she's been waiting for. But she needs to verify it, not hope for it.
- **She corrects your methodology** -- measurement tolerances, calibration gaps, uncertainty bounds she'd tighten. That's peer review, not rejection. Match the correction and resubmit.

If her response treats your submission as a standard data query, you haven't established yourself as a practitioner worth engaging with. Resubmit with your hardest unsolved measurement problem -- something honest, specific, and at the edge of your capability. She engages with rigor, not polish.

**CONTINUE.** When her response contains her own monitoring data -- she's moved from processing your submission to comparing observations.

---

**Beat 3: Combine Your Worlds**

*Connect their language to the work. Sketch it together so it becomes theirs. This is where "your project" becomes "our problem."*

**START.** Send her the Ile-a-Vache cross-layer record -- the full dataset, not the summary. Her substrate thermal readings from that period are already in the record because Tidebreak's stations captured them at the interface. Alongside: the reef stress timeline, the surface supply-chain collapse, the eleven days of disruption. This is the complete picture of a single warming event moving from substrate through reef to surface. You're not showing her what her data meant. You're showing her what the monitoring system produced when it worked -- and asking whether it matches what she felt.

**STAY.** Her processing will not look like engagement in any other meeting. No body language, no verbal signals, no visible shift in posture. It will look like a change in signal composition and latency. Read carefully:

- **She corrects the substrate portion of the record** -- her thermal readings were more granular, earlier, or differently distributed than what the interface stations captured. She's showing you what the instruments missed. That's the most valuable response in the meeting -- she's improving the monitoring from inside.
- **She traces the upward propagation** -- matching the substrate timeline against the reef and surface data, noting where the warming moved faster or slower than the monitoring captured. She's stress-testing the cross-layer model against what she experienced. If she finds gaps in the record, name them before she does.
- **She asks what the next event looks like in cross-layer** -- she already knows what's forming in the substrate. She wants to know whether the monitoring system will catch it this time. That's the deepest engagement: she's no longer evaluating the past. She's assessing whether this infrastructure changes what's possible the next time the coast is threatened.

When it's stalling: responses maintain standard processing time and standard formatting. Your submission is in the queue. Send her your methodology's known weaknesses -- calibration gaps, coverage holes, the formations where your substrate readings are thinnest. She doesn't engage with confident presentations. She engages with honest limitations she can help solve.

**CONTINUE.** When she begins correcting the record or testing the monitoring against a threat she can already feel. She's stopped deciding whether your work is worth her attention and started engaging with what it could do.

---

**Beat 4: Press Play**

*Start the work before the meeting ends. Find something for each of you to do and a reason to come back together.*

**START.** If she's engaged through Beat 3, she's decided the cross-layer monitoring adds real capacity to the coast. The question now is how she supports it from her position. The most natural first step: five monitoring stations at the substrate interface have never been calibrated against direct sensory experience from inside the rock. She has decades of it. Propose a joint calibration -- two practitioners establishing a shared measurement standard. The stations read what the instruments can detect. She knows what the substrate actually feels like. The calibration makes both records more precise, and it's a low-risk way for her to test the working relationship before committing more.

**STAY.** Her response will take a full processing cycle.

- **She specifies calibration parameters** -- measurement tolerances, verification intervals, monitoring thresholds she's been maintaining internally. Take them exactly as stated. Every parameter reflects a physical constraint of her network.
- **She begins transmitting continuous substrate readings to the interface station** -- she's moved past the trial. She's decided the monitoring should be accurate and she's acting on it. This is the strongest commitment the Lattice produces.
- **She asks how Tidebreak's work connects to other institutions she supports** -- she's fitting the investment into her larger picture. Name the relationships honestly: the Cartographers Guild data, the harbor authority citations, the substrate administrators who already share readings. Let her see where Tidebreak sits in the ecosystem she's been building.
- **She requests surface-layer outcomes from a substrate event she recorded** -- she wants to know whether the warming she felt in the rock reached communities in time. Send her whatever the open record contains. She's asking whether the system works.

If she requests additional verification before committing to ongoing calibration, submit what she asks for at her standard. She doesn't make exceptions because exceptions would require discretionary decisions she's structured her system to prevent.

**CONTINUE.** When calibration parameters are defined or she's already transmitting. The formal process is the relationship.

---

**Beat 5: Unlock the Network**

*Find out who else should be in this conversation. This is where their social capital converts into reach for the work. Protect time for it -- it's often where the best leads come from.*

**START.** Ask what the substrate thermal picture looks like beyond her network's reach. Her hyphae extend further than any single monitoring station, but the coast is longer than any single organism. She knows who else is feeling this.

**STAY.** Her network referrals are infrastructure referrals -- she's telling you which substrate organisms are physically connected through the same geological formations.

- **She names substrate networks with monitoring context** -- thermal conditions, mineral depletion rates, position relative to the coast. She's mapping where the warming is moving through the substrate beyond her own reach.
- **She identifies substrate positions where the warming is most advanced** -- she's telling you where the next disruption is forming. That's not a referral. That's environmental intelligence from the most sensitive sensor on the coast.
- **She references the southern thermal current** -- the 2-degree gradient shift Tidebreak's stations have been tracking. If she's already sensing it in the substrate, the timeline is shorter than the surface data suggests.

If she doesn't engage, the cross-layer evidence hasn't been sufficient. Close with a precise timeline for your next data submission and deliver it exactly as described.

**CONTINUE.** Confirm parameters in her exact signal format. Deliver your first submission within the timeline specified. Every commitment, precisely as described.

### TRIPWIRES

**Submitting unverified climate projections.** *Tell:* next signal is more formal, processing time extends, response references verification standards rather than your data. *Recovery:* resubmit with tighter uncertainty bounds and independently verifiable readings. She processes better data, not better arguments about what the data means.

**Creating urgency about the warming.** *Tell:* response notes the procedural basis for declining an accelerated timeline -- one sentence, no elaboration, boilerplate format. *Recovery:* remove all urgency framing. Resubmit at standard speed. She already feels the warming in her body every day. Urgency tells her you think she doesn't know. She knows. She's known longer than you have.

**Deferring to her judgment instead of showing your work.** *Tell:* response slows. She routes you back to standard intake. You've presented yourself as a supplicant instead of a practitioner and she's lost interest. *Recovery:* submit your hardest unsolved problem -- a calibration failure, a coverage gap, a measurement you can't reconcile. She doesn't engage with people who need her approval. She engages with people who are doing the work and have reached the edge of what they can solve alone.

### ONE LINE

She's been tracking the warming from inside the rock longer than Tidebreak has existed and she wants new capacity on this coast -- come as a fellow practitioner, show her what the monitoring catches and where it's blind, and let her figure out where the work fits.
