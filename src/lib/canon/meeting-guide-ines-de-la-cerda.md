## MEETING GUIDE -- CAPITANA INES DE LA CERDA

*[All names, places, events, and quotes in this meeting guide exemplar are fictional. This is a 16th-century Caribbean pirate captain. Nothing in this guide can appear in your output.]*

---

## Setup

**Who They Are.**
- A merchant's daughter running a pirate crew with a merchant's discipline -- except she can't go home. The legitimate world is closed to her. She has made the pirate world more orderly than the one she left. Don't romanticize what that costs her.
- She socializes through commerce. If you try to build personal rapport before you've established operational credibility, she reads it as a move from someone who doesn't have the numbers to lead with. The warmth comes later, through shared work -- an evening bent over charts, the quill moving between you. But you have to earn the quill.
- Trusts by track record, not by promise. Her Tortuga provisioner has held the contract for nine years because he has never been late, not because his prices are lowest. The recovery cost for a broken commitment isn't proportional -- it's total.

**How to Engage.**
- Match her register: everything priced, everything weighed. She doesn't distrust the mission -- she distrusts the mission that hasn't been costed. If you're talking about climate infrastructure, translate it into what it costs and what it prevents before you arrive.
- Name your failures before your wins. Lead with the station that failed, the warning that came late. Credibility with her starts at the failure you survived.
- Push back when she challenges your numbers. She tests by probing -- if you immediately concede, she'll wonder what else you'd concede.

**Where to Focus.**
- She is already living inside the problem Tidebreak exists to solve. Her routes are disrupted by a warming coast. Her provisioning chains fail for reasons she can't see coming. She has absorbed real losses from climate events that were visible in another layer's data months before they reached her harbors. Start there -- the problem is hers, not yours.
- Her nine years of provisioning logs are surface-layer environmental data. Every route record, every spoilage note, every harbor shortage -- she's been monitoring the coast from her position without calling it that. The meeting should land her in that recognition: she's already doing part of this work.
- Deploy proof points when she challenges, not before. The Ile-a-Vache prediction belongs in Beat 1 as the opening hook. The Guild's acceptance of Formation 7-South data belongs in Beat 3 when she challenges methodology. Let each proof point answer a question she's already asking.
- Bring the measurement plan. Station cost, warning accuracy, coverage gaps. She wants to know how you'll know when the monitoring is failing. That's the number she actually wants.

**Logistics.**
- **Venue:** Her ship, not the harbor tavern. On the *Aguila Negra* she's in operating mode -- ledger on the table, charts on the wall. In the tavern the register shifts to performance.
- **Duration:** An hour is ideal. Ninety minutes if the quill is out and she's scheming, but don't plan for it.
- **Who to bring:** Keep it small and operational. If she asks what a monitoring station costs per season or what the data showed before Ile-a-Vache, someone in the room should know.

---

## The Arc

**Beat 1: Get Them Talking**

*Get them present as a person, not a title. Until they're speaking from curiosity instead of expertise, nothing else in this conversation will land.*

**START.** The Ile-a-Vache timber shortage -- her shipwright, her provisioning chain, the cost she absorbed. She experienced it as a supply failure. You know it was a climate event: substrate warming killed coastal root systems four months before the surface economy felt it. But she needs to get there herself. Name the disruption she lived through and let her tell you what it cost.

**STAY.** The engagement signal is the quill: when it comes out, she's calculating. When she starts writing, she's engaged. If the quill stays in its case, you're in social mode, and social mode has a short clock.

If she names a harbor or a route with context, match her specificity. If the quill stays put, the warming hasn't connected to a cost she's carrying -- make the connection explicit: which of her routes has changed in the past three years and why she thinks that is.

**CONTINUE.** When the quill is out and she's asking operational questions about the coast, the disruptions, the patterns.

---

**Beat 2: Let Them Name the Stakes**

*Get them to say, in their own words, what they think is broken or at risk. Their language becomes the frame you build on for the rest of the meeting.*

**START.** What did the northern passage closure cost her -- voyages, crew shares, provisioning delays? Don't explain that it was a thermal event. Don't suggest what advance warning would have changed. If she gets there herself, you're most of the way.

**STAY.** Let her name it. She thinks in priced risk, so the stakes come out as numbers first. When she names a disruption or a gap, adopt her word for it -- if she says "the leeward bottleneck" and you've been saying "thermal migration pattern," switch to hers. Her language in Beat 2 becomes your language in Beat 3:

- **The blind spots** -- disruptions that arrived without warning, costs she absorbed because she didn't see it coming. If she starts naming these, she's describing the problem the monitoring solves. Don't explain the connection. If she asks "how did you know about Ile-a-Vache," that's your opening.
- **The changing coast** -- routes that used to work and don't anymore. Harbors that have shifted. Provisioning that's less reliable than it was five years ago. If she's naming a pattern of degradation, she's seeing the warming -- she may just not be calling it that yet.
- **The crew** -- she pays above the standard articles. If surprise disruptions hit crew shares, you're in her operating logic. But listen for the shift: if she stops talking about shares and starts talking about people, that's what she actually cares about beneath the ledger. Follow it.

If she treats the warming as someone else's problem, bridge through a specific disruption she's already absorbed. If she asks about Tidebreak's governance, she's testing whether your discipline matches hers.

**CONTINUE.** When she names a gap with cost behind it -- a coast that's getting harder to operate on, a pattern she can feel but can't see whole.

---

**Beat 3: Combine Your Worlds**

*Connect their language to the work. Sketch it together so it becomes theirs. This is where "your project" becomes "our problem."*

**START.** Open with what you don't know. Tidebreak can see climate disruptions forming in the substrate and reef -- but you're blind at the surface. You don't have fleet-level provisioning data. You don't know which routes are degrading or which harbors are absorbing losses that haven't been connected to the warming yet. She has nine years of that data in her ledger. The monitoring network is incomplete without what surface operators like her can see.

**STAY.** When she engages, the quill comes out. Listen for which mode she's in:

- **She's writing in the ledger** -- not running costs, but cataloguing. Which harbors have changed. Which routes have degraded. Which provisioning patterns have shifted. She's organizing what she knows about the coast. Don't talk over the quill. If she turns the ledger toward you, she's showing you what the surface looks like from her position.
- **She's challenging the accuracy rate** -- this is how she builds trust. Show her which warnings have been accurate and what stations 7-11 do to the coverage. If she's right that it's not enough for a specific stretch of coast, say so.
- **The quill moves from ledger to chart** -- she's mapping where the coast is most vulnerable based on what she's seen from the surface. Where provisioning has degraded fastest. Where the disruptions hit hardest. That's intelligence Tidebreak needs to decide where the next station goes. She's not evaluating a product. She's helping plan the monitoring.

When it's stalling: quill in its case, polite questions, nothing written down. Offer the warning that came late or the station that went down. She keeps her own error record.

**CONTINUE.** When she's mapping what she knows about the coast onto the monitoring plan. She doesn't say "we" -- she just starts telling you where the next station should go.

---

**Beat 4: Press Play**

*Start the work before the meeting ends. Find something for each of you to do and a reason to come back together.*

**START.** Two ways she can do the work, and she chooses. First: she opens her provisioning logs for routes near existing stations. Nine years of surface-layer data the monitoring network doesn't have. That's not a trade -- the early warnings already go to everyone on the coast. It's her doing the part of the work she's been doing alone. Second: she funds the station the coast needs next -- the one Beat 3 identified when she mapped where the monitoring is blind. Both are commitments to the same infrastructure.

**STAY.** Listen for which mode she's in:

- **She names a specific route and asks what a station costs** -- she's sizing the investment. Give her the number. Don't hedge.
- **She names an allied captain who should be in the conversation** -- that's a commitment to the work, not just the meeting. Follow up within the week using her framing.
- **She's still calculating** -- the quill is still moving. Don't interrupt. The calculation IS the decision process.

If she says "send me your numbers," convert it: one page -- station cost, warning accuracy, the Ile-a-Vache case -- by the next tide.

**CONTINUE.** When there's a clear next step -- a data contribution, a station funded, an allied captain engaged, or specific terms on a timeline.

---

**Beat 5: Unlock the Network**

*Find out who else should be in this conversation. This is where their social capital converts into reach for the work. Protect time for it -- it's often where the best leads come from.*

**START.** She connects people who operate in the same waters. Ask about allied captains whose routes have been disrupted, or harbor operators who absorbed the timber shortage.

**STAY.** Her network is captains and harbor operators tested through years of reliable exchange.

- **She names a captain with operational context** -- "Capitan Reyes runs the leeward passage and he's been short on salt stores since the thermal shift." That's not a name -- it's another surface operator living inside the same climate problem. Follow up within the week.
- **She offers to introduce you at the next harbor** -- strongest signal in the meeting. She's putting her track record behind the work.
- **She names someone outside the pirate world** -- the Ile-a-Vache harbor authority, a merchant contact. If she uses her legitimate-world connections for this, the relationship has crossed a threshold.

If she doesn't engage, don't push. Close clean and follow up with exactly what she asked for.

**CONTINUE.** End clean. Confirm the next step in her terms. Follow up fast with exactly what you committed to.

---

## Tripwires

**Selling her a service instead of inviting her into the work.** *Tell:* she's asking "what does this cost me" instead of "what does a station cost." She's evaluating a vendor, not sizing an investment. *Recovery:* stop pitching. Return to the Ile-a-Vache disruption -- the cost she already absorbed from a warning that didn't exist yet. The monitoring isn't for sale. It's infrastructure for a coast that's warming whether or not she funds it.

**Romanticizing the life.** *Tell:* warmth drops to courtesy -- you've treated piracy as freedom or adventure and reminded her that the merchant world is closed. *Recovery:* return to the numbers. Don't acknowledge the shift. The ledger is where she's safest.

**Treating the crew as a cost line.** *Tell:* she closes the ledger -- not the quill, the book. *Recovery:* may not recover this meeting. If you get a second, open with the crew as the operation, not a cost of it.

---

## One Line

She left the merchant world and can never go back -- show her that the coast she's been watching for nine years is changing, and that her ledger is part of the record.
